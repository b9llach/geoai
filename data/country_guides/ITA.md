## general_clues

Contrary to most European licence plates, Italian plates have blue strips on either side. Furthermore, the front plate is distinctly shorter than most European plates. NOTE: Albanian licence plates also have two blue strips, French plates have a smaller strip on the right. However, neither Albanian nor French front plates are short.

Italian bollards are triangular in shape, white with a black top. Red front and white rear reflector. NOTE: Albania uses the same bollards.

One of the most common types of Italian road lines: white solid outer lines with no middle line. Generally less common in other Mediterranean countries.

Italian uses many double consonants and typically has a very large number of i's. You can find ì, é and à. Most words end with a vowel. The Italian word for street is 'via', found on most street signs.

Italy has a Mediterranean landscape with agricultural fields on rolling hills and forested mountain valleys.

Italian architecture mainly consists of pastel coloured stone houses, generally with terracotta roofs. Houses commonly have wooden shutters, typically divided in two and opening sideways.

Unlike most Mediterranean countries, Italy features a lot of round concrete poles.

Wooden poles in Italy commonly feature a small white pole sticker.

The most common pole tops in Italy are concrete trident pole tops.

It is very common to find double guardrails in Italy.

Italian guardrails feature red front reflectors. NOTE: Distinguishes Italy from Spain (yellow reflectors).

Italian guardrails commonly end in a distinct shape — flatter, wider, bending away from the road.

It is very common for the back of Italian road signs to be black or dark grey.

Directional signs are blue with a white arrow. All letters capitalised. National roads (Strada Statale): blue with white and blue border. Divided highways (Autostrade): green signs with white and green border.

Italian kilometre markers are white squares with a roman numeral on top and an arabic numeral on the bottom (kilometres + hundred metres from start of road).

Italian road numbers normally begin with letters "SS", "SR" or "SP". Second letter: "S" = State road (unique countrywide); "R" = Regional (unique to region); "P" = Provincial (unique to province).

Italian chevrons are white arrows on a black background. NOTE: In Southern Europe, white on black chevrons can also be found in Spain, Greece and Albania.

Street signs are typically white with a thin blue border and black text.

Italian pedestrian signs have five stripes and a basic person without a belt.

Signs featuring a "no parking" sign with the text "passo carrabile" are commonly found in urban areas. The municipality (comune) name is typically at the top.

Italy has Generation 4 "smallcam" coverage.

## regional_clues

Italian area codes follow a pattern where the first digit generally increases to the east, then south.

Each Italian region has its own unique flag.

Languages: South Tyrol has a German majority, uses German bilingual signs.

French can be seen on signs in the Aosta Valley. Street signs often use both Italian "via" and French "rue".

Landscapes & Vegetation: The Padan Plain (Po Valley) typically recognized by complete flatness. Has some of the worst air quality in Europe, leading to noticeable haze in coverage.

The southeastern peninsula surrounding Bari has many olive plantations, often seen with bright rock walls. Dirt typically has a noticeably red colour. NOTE: Similar landscape can also be found in southeastern Sicily.

The alpine region in northern Italy has tall, wide mountain ranges and deep valleys.

Sicilian landscape: arid, hilly agricultural landscape, often with rocky mountains.

Grey rocks sticking up from the ground are commonly found on Sardinia, particularly in the northeast. NOTE: Similar phenomenon on Sicily, but landscape will typically be much drier than Sardinia.

Cacti are most common in the south, particularly in Sicily, southern Sardinia and the south of Apulia.

Citrus plantations are primarily on eastern Sicily and the southern tip of Calabria.

Rice mainly produced in western and central Po valley, less commonly in Sardinia.

Architecture: Many buildings in and around Tuscany are made of irregular unpainted brown-grey bricks. NOTE: Similar in northeastern Spain.

Alpine architecture (large buildings with white stone walls and wooden details) found in Alpine region.

Buildings in Aosta Valley often built with grey uneven bricks, Alpine look.

Solid wooden shutters with black metal hinges are common in the northeast, particularly Veneto.

Houses in the Apulian region are generally very light coloured, with flat roofs, typically with two to three floors. Sometimes reminiscent of the Middle East.

The trullo style of roof is unique to the Itria valley in southeastern Italy. Particularly common in/around Alberobello.

## spotlight

Close to the Slovenian border you can find Slovene bilingual signs.

The Campione d'Italia exclave in Switzerland can be recognized by its low camera, long antenna and a flag. Body of water to the west.

Etna, the tallest volcano in Europe, can be seen rising above the landscape from far away in eastern Sicily.

The volcano of Vesuvius can often be seen to the south or east from the plains around Naples.

Venice can typically be recognized by its many canals running through most of the city.

Plana del Fùcino is a small plain surrounded by mountains in the middle of Italy with a grid of roads going completely north-to-south.

The island of Pantelleria is a volcanic island southwest of Sicily. Dark volcanic rocks visible in many rock walls and on some houses. Yellow lichen covering the rocks. Very large amount of cacti.

The Aeolian islands are a volcanic archipelago north of Sicily — mix of volcanic soil/rocks with mediterranean vegetation, like stone pines or olive trees. Cacti fairly common. Touristic white villas common in the mountains.

Within the Aeolian islands, Salina can easily be distinguished from the twin volcanoes forming a valley across the island.

Vulcano (Aeolian) — its volcano has an almost white stripe of dirt following the top of the mountainside.

The island of Favignana has a distinct topography with mountains running north to south splitting the island in two. Many rock walls similar to those in Bari. Not volcanic.

The island of San Domino only has overcast Generation 2 coverage, mostly looking quite gloomy.
