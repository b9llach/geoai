## general_clues

Costa Rica's Generation 4 imagery was captured using a large white pickup truck. In many locations, it is either fully or partially visible, but can occasionally be entirely blurred out. There is an antenna attached to the front of the car. NOTE: The Panamanian truck has a distinctive white metal frame behind the driver's cabin and a fully blurred out car is more common there. The truck used in Ecuador does not have an antenna.

Costa Rican licence plates are typically short and white with blue text, giving them a light blue tint through the blur.

Costa Rica is a mostly lush tropical country, with rolling hills, agricultural lowlands, peaky volcanoes running through the centre, and dense rainforests dotted around.

Costa Rican architecture is less colonial than Panama. Few houses have proper tiled roofs — dark red sheet metal or dark red plastic tiles are more common. Buildings typically have pastel-coloured concrete walls and are usually a low single-family house or boxy two-storey structure. Often heavily guarded with fences and barred windows.

Like in most of Latin America, Spanish is the official language of Costa Rica.

Like in Mexico, Panama and Guatemala, Costa Rican stop signs display the word "ALTO". NOTE: Stop signs in Panama use a thicker font. In the rest of Spanish Latin America they say "PARE".

Red taxis are the standard in Costa Rica. NOTE: Panama uses yellow taxis.

Costa Rica uses long silver insulators. NOTE: Panama uses a variety of insulators (long silver, short silver, long black, UFO, white mushroom). Brazil can have long silver insulators. Ecuador uses UFO and mushroom insulators.

One of the most common pole tops is an even crossbar with uneven insulators (one on one side, two on the other). Crossbar is further down on the pole than usual. NOTE: Poles in Costa Rica usually feature support bars, while this is much more rare on Panamanian poles.

Poles with a single insulator attached to the side at the top of the pole are commonly found in Costa Rica.

Utility poles in Costa Rica feature a lamp with a blue dot on the top, similarly to other Latin American countries.

Also common is a thin metal crossbar bent 90 degrees to form an L-shape. NOTE: Also commonly found in Panama, Colombia and Ecuador.

Many road signs have a distinct cross on the back. Most common on diamond-shaped warning signs.

The equivalent of a bollard in Costa Rica would be vertical orange boards with 3 black circles on them.

Electricity counters in Costa Rica are circular and often have a big metal box alongside them.

Imperial beer ads with its black eagle mascot are a common sight in Costa Rica.

## regional_clues

The provinces are sometimes written on signs. Each province has their own provincial flag.

Area codes are moderately useful to learn for Costa Rica.

Infrastructure: Poles are sometimes marked with large black or white stickers, containing multiple lines of text. The first digit of the topmost number is province-specific.

Asymmetrical pole tops with one insulator on one side of the pole and two on the other are found in and around San José.

Pole attachments consisting of numbers made out of wires are found in central Alajuela and over the border in Heredia province.

White pole plates with black lettering are specific to the Greater Metropolitan Area.

Blue lettering forming a P-XXXXX code with or without the accompanying white background can be found in western Cartago province.

Small black stickers with a QR code on the bottom can be seen around Corralillo, south of San José.

Yellow pole paint that is tall with 3 sections is mainly found in Puntarenas and southern Alajuela provinces.

Yellow pole paint that is shorter with all numbers without dividing lines is mainly found in Guanacaste and northwestern Alajuela provinces.

Yellow pole paint divided into 2 sections with horizontal characters at the bottom are almost unique to southern Alajuela province.

Black pole stickers with yellow lettering, located near the bottom of the pole, are limited to western Guanacaste province and the Nicoya peninsula.

Landscape: North of the mountains lie the largely flat Caribbean Lowlands, characterised by extensive agriculture, grey dirt and a good number of straight roads.

A lot of the coverage in western Costa Rica was captured during the dry season.

Most banana plantations are found in Limón province.

Oil palm plantations are mainly along the coast in Puntarenas province; smaller plantations in Limón province.

Pineapple plantations can be seen in western Limón province, northern Alajuela province, and around Buenos Aires in Puntarenas province.

Sugarcane plantations: in the southern area around San Isidro de El General and Buenos Aires you can find sugarcane plantations in bloom with silvery tufts of strands at the top.

Rice fields are mostly found south of Bagaces in Guanacaste province.

The majority of coffee plantations are located throughout the central valley.

Cassava plantations are most commonly found in western Limón province.

Teak (recognisable for tall straight trunks and round glossy yellow-green leaves) is most common in Guanacaste.

Guanacaste trees are recognised by their thick trunk and characteristically large, umbrella-shaped crown. Mostly seen in eponymous Guanacaste province.

Attalea palms (mainly along southern coast) recognised by upwards facing leaves that droop slightly.

Macaw palms are common in western Costa Rica with long, disheveled looking broods forming an almost round crown, often with spiky trunk.

Dark volcanic rocks can be found in most of central Costa Rica, but more common around Guapiles, Limón Province and around Miravalles Volcano.

Miscellaneous: Taxis in Costa Rica are red, featuring yellow triangles on sides and top containing the city/town name as well as a letter code denoting the province.

San José is the capital and by far the largest urban area. Situated in a valley between multiple mountains, the roads have a noticeable incline.

Simple fences made of irregular wooden branches or logs are found all over Costa Rica but much more prevalent in the west.

Larger irrigation canals exist in the area south of Bagaces and Cañas in Guanacaste province.

Houses on stilts are mostly found on the northeastern coast.

The antenna can occasionally be seen folded down, predominantly in the north and northeast of the country.

## spotlight

The entire municipality of San José consistently uses green street signs with a white rectangle containing an emblem. Calle/Avenida streets are numerically ordered, increasing from downtown outwards.

The only concrete highways are the eastern stretch of Ruta Nacional 18, and the divided Pan-American Highway (RN 1) from Liberia to the intersection with RN 18.

Another major divided highway is RN 32 from intersection with RN 4 to Limón on the Caribbean coast. Passes through lush forests in the generally flat northern lowlands.

From the "Route 32 Toll" close to the San José and Heredia border until where it intersects RN 4 west of Guapiles, the road uses yellow bollards placed between the centre road lines.

A yellow follow car with two front windows can be seen trailing the Google truck on RN 2 east of Palmar Norte and halfway up to Buenos Aires.

Wind turbines are found in clusters, mainly in Guanacaste province, but also southwest of Cartago.

Bright white soil roads place you just north of Liberia, Guanacaste province.

Late evening into night coverage can be found in San Vito.

Tuk-Tuks can be seen around Puerto Viejo de Talamanca on the northeast coast, and less often around Nosara on the west coast.

Trekkers: Generation 3 coverage in Costa Rica is very limited, consisting of trekkers in and around San José and by the northern west coast. Cocos Island (500 km southwest of coast) and a boat trekker around Caño Island. Beach trekkers are mostly on the northern east coast. Most rural forest trekkers are found in central Costa Rica.

The Children's Museum in northern San José can be recognised by its grand castle-like exterior.

The University of Costa Rica, in eastern San José, is fairly busy, with a lot of greenery and various medium-sized buildings.

Palacio de los Deportes is a stadium in Heredia, recognised by its boxy look combined with red, blue and white colour scheme.

At the Juan Santamaría International Airport, the trekker walks in the middle of a major four-lane road.

The Cartago Municipal Museum can be recognised by its palace-like facade with a neatly planted garden in front, as well as the railway in the middle of the street.

The Ruins of Cartago is the remains of a large Catholic church near the centre.

The Punta Leona Resort can be recognised by red brick pavement.

Most coverage in Filadelfia de Guanacaste was taken on the edge of a park, with distinct red brick pavement where the bricks are placed in the shape of an "L".
