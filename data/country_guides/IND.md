## general_clues

The vast majority of the coverage in India was taken with "shitcam", a low-quality camera typically recognised by brownish colours and often a large circular blur covering the Street View car. NOTE: Within Asia, Nepal is also entirely "shitcam"; common in Cambodia and rare in Sri Lanka and Bangladesh.

All Generation 4 coverage in India was taken using smallcam, mounted lower than regular Generation 4 with a relatively big circular blur with a small protrusion at the front.

While most of India is covered in shitcam, there is also a significant amount of Generation 3 trekkers throughout the country.

India drives on the left side of the road.

A wide variety of scripts can be found in India. Most of them can be used to narrow down the region.

English is also an official language and can be seen on a lot of signage.

Indian licence plates are typically fairly long. Most common: white. Commercial: yellow plates. Electric: green.

India typically has black and white striped signposts. NOTE: Bangladesh, Nepal and Sri Lanka also have black and white signposts.

The most common Indian utility pole is a concrete square pole with a trident pole top. Many regions have their own distinct poles. NOTE: Indian poles will almost always be made of concrete or metal. Wooden poles are extremely uncommon.

## regional_clues

Languages: Hindi is most common in central and northern regions. Uses Devanagari script — line through top of each word, straight vertical lines.

Marathi (Maharashtra) also uses Devanagari, sometimes uses ळ which is not in Hindi.

Punjabi (Punjab in northwestern India) — softer letter shapes, horizontal bar broken over certain letters. Distinctive: ਨ, ਲ, ਅ.

Gujarati (Gujarat) similar to Hindi but lacks the horizontal line at the top of every word.

Bengali (northeastern India) similar to Devanagari but with left-facing triangular shapes. Same as Bangladesh.

Assamese (Assam) uses Bengali script with additions like ৰ and ৱ.

Kannada (Karnataka) — smooth curves and small circles. Similar to Telugu. Many characters have a squiggly tail in the top right corner.

Telugu (Telangana, Andhra Pradesh) — similar to Kannada but with diacritics resembling checkmarks at the top of letters.

Tamil (Tamil Nadu) — distinct combination of curves, straight lines and 90° angles. Some letters have dot-shaped diacritics. Also common in Sri Lanka.

Oriya (Odisha) — curvy, many letters have wide semi-circles covering the top. Mnemonic: shape of characters resembles a skull emoji.

Malayalam (Kerala) — simple-looking curvy letters, often containing upside-down U-shapes.

Manipuri/Meitei (Manipur) — many vertical lines and right angles.

Mizo (Mizoram) — uses Latin script.

Meghalaya almost exclusively uses English.

Infrastructure: Yellow tuk tuks with black roofs are common in Telangana, Andhra Pradesh, Tamil Nadu.

Concrete holey poles are unique to Gujarat and the Union territory of Daman and Diu, and Dadra and Nagar Haveli. Rarely in Maharashtra, Madhya Pradesh, Chhattisgarh.

Poles divided in two sections (Brazilian-style) are common in Andhra Pradesh and Telangana.

Metal poles with a mesh pattern are commonly found in Sikkim.

Metal holey poles are common in Kerala.

Thin grey metal poles are seen all over northeastern India and the Himalayan region of Uttarakhand and Himachal Pradesh.

Electricity counters in Punjab are usually mint green with small window-like squares.

Small metal poletops shaped like a window are found in Punjab and Haryana.

In Karnataka (and sometimes Haryana), trident poletops where central insulator is attached directly to the top while the others are on a horizontal crossbar slightly further down.

In Manipur, trident poletops on poles made entirely of metal.

Upside down triangle poletops placed below the top of the pole are commonly found in Maharashtra, Chhattisgarh, less commonly in Madhya Pradesh.

Poles in Madhya Pradesh often have a metal bar shaped like an upside down A slightly below the top.

A connector on the pole shaped like an upside down trapezoid is most common in Chhattisgarh, somewhat common in Meghalaya, Tripura, Maharashtra.

Poletops attached with two vertical bars forming a tall narrow rectangle are found in West Bengal.

In West Bengal, poletops with two crossbars connected with thin vertical supports.

In the northeast, combination of even and uneven poletops on the same pole.

Poles with two diagonal bars forming a '<' shape are common in Meghalaya and Arunachal Pradesh, occasionally Assam.

In Assam, Meghalaya and Mizoram, poles with two crossbars each with two insulators. More than two bars suggests Meghalaya or Mizoram.

Trident poletops with the tip of the middle bar bent at 90° angle in Assam.

Poletops with a short crossbar above a longer crossbar in Goa.

Pole markings with one black stripe between two white ones are common in Tamil Nadu.

Pole paint with five or more thin black and yellow stripes in Delhi.

Pole paint with exactly four thicker black and yellow stripes in Bihar.

Trees with four red and white stripes near the bottom in Bihar (less commonly Jharkhand).

Small yellow pole markings are commonly found in Kerala.

Architecture: Rounded roof tiles are common in central India, often appearing disorganised.

Roofs in southern India have organised, bright orange flat tiles with a ridge in the middle. Particularly common in Tamil Nadu.

Flat roof tiles with a small diamond shaped protrusion in middle in West Bengal.

Large hip roofs of corrugated sheet metal in West Bengal and adjacent areas.

Metal roofs with a small triangle near the top are common in Northeast India.

Houses made of square panels separated by thin beams in Northeast India.

Houses or huts with thatched roofs are mainly along east coast and northern plains.

Gurdwaras (white domes, ornate walls) are most common in Punjab.

Landscapes & Vegetation: Chir pines are more or less exclusive to Uttarakhand, Himachal Pradesh, Jammu and Kashmir, westernmost Arunachal Pradesh.

Khasi pines are mainly found in Meghalaya and Manipur.

Cacti (Euphorbia neriifolia) commonly part of fences in Gujarat, western Maharashtra, southern half of Rajasthan.

Palmyra Palms (round crown, fan shaped leaves, lollipop shape) — south or east of country.

Areca Palms (very thin trunk, small crown) — southwestern coast, northeast, Andaman and Nicobar Islands.

Date palms in drier central regions.

Oil palm plantations most common in Andhra Pradesh panhandle, especially near eastern Telangana border.

Tea plantations in northeast and far south. In flat area = likely Assam or northeastern West Bengal.

Sugarcane most common in Uttar Pradesh and the southwest.

Cotton commonly found in western parts (Gujarat, Maharashtra).

Canola mostly in and around Rajasthan.

Tallest mountains in India are in the Himalayas.

In Ladakh and northern Himachal Pradesh: extremely dry landscape with little to no vegetation within Himalayan mountains.

Northern plains (Ganges River Valley): very fertile with extremely flat and open agricultural fields.

Distinctly dark soil mainly in Maharashtra and adjacent regions.

Red soil in several regions, mainly southern west coast and Chhattisgarh and western Odisha.

Western Rajasthan: dry landscape with sandy soil.

Western Ghats Mountains in western Maharashtra — distinct ridgelines, often plateaus rather than peaks.

Mizoram landscape: lush green valleys with low vegetation, eroded hillsides with many small peaks.

Miscellaneous: Large visible square shaped cars in specific regions.

In the northeast, fences made of thin wooden sticks in a square pattern.

Buddhist prayer flags in Sikkim and Arunachal Pradesh.

## spotlight

On the Andaman and Nicobar Islands, black pole stickers containing several digits and an arrow in the middle.

Chandigarh is a planned industrial city — blue signs mentioning sectors and industrial areas, round yellow markings on street lights, street signs typically featuring numbers rather than street names.

Orange and bright green buses are typically found in Delhi.

Large roads in Ahmedabad and Surat have separated middle lanes for buses.

Black taxis with yellow roofs in Meghalaya and Mumbai.

Taxis in Sikkim often have a painted yellow hood or a small yellow circle on it.

Elevated railways in: Bengaluru, Chennai, Delhi, Hyderabad, Jaipur, Kochi, Kolkata, Mumbai.

The area on the border of Telangana and Karnataka has houses with roofs made of thin grey bricks.

White painted clay huts in northwestern Bihar and northeastern Uttar Pradesh.

Wooden huts with a cross pattern in central Bihar.

Japanese cedar trees (significant height, narrow fluffy crowns) most commonly found near Sikkim/West Bengal border, and in Meghalaya.

The Himalayan cedar most commonly seen on NH34 from Harsil to Gangotri, and around Shimla.

The Bhutan white pine (hanging needles all pointing down) mainly in Mechuka Valley and near Bhutan border in Arunachal Pradesh.

Doum palms almost exclusive to the union territory of Diu.

The endangered Indian pitcher plant exclusive to the Khasi Hills (mainly Meghalaya).

Western half of Uttarakhand is far more barren than other Himalayan regions due to logging and erosion.

The Mechuka valley is the only place in Northeast India where snowy mountains are visible. Brownish grass and Bhutan white pine.

Chaukhamba near Ukhimath: mountain massif covered in snow with three distinct peaks, centre tallest.

Landscape south of Shillong in Meghalaya: V-shaped, green eroded valleys with somewhat flat smooth ridgetops.

Salt flats and coastal marshes with nearly no vegetation in parts of Gujarat.

Near southern tip of Tamil Nadu, large monolithic mountains with huge exposed rocks.

Flat, bushy landscape with very red soil in southern Tamil Nadu.

Lakshadweep (off coast of Kerala) easily recognised by abundance of coconut trees, often painted red-and-white.

Coverage on road NH326 west of Brahmapur in Odisha has a thin grey antenna. Generally very high-quality for shitcam.

Parts of roads NH6 and NH206 southeast of Shillong recognised by khasi pines with distinct yellow outer road lines and fresh dark pavement.
