## general_clues

Licence plates in Brazil were white until 2018. Now, a white plate with a blue stripe on top is used. Red is still used for commercial vehicles.

Portuguese is spoken in Brazil, as opposed to most Latin American countries which speak Spanish. Compared to Spanish, some essential differences are that Portuguese uses -ão rather than -ón, -ch/-lh instead of -ll and -nh instead of -ñ.

Soil in most of Brazil is distinctly red. It can range from a light pink around Mato Grosso to a deep maroon near the Paraná river. The soil is usually not red in the North East, or in the majority of Rio Grande do Sul. NOTE: Parts of Paraguay and the nearby Argentine province of Misiones have deep red soil.

The most common poles in Brazil have some resemblance to a ladder in the bottom portion. They are rectangular, have long segments running through the bottom, and small holes near the top. NOTE: Paraguay uses similar poles.

Most Brazilian road signs have the backs painted black. NOTE: This is also common in Paraguay.

Blue road markers with the road number and kilometre can be seen on the sides of roads.

Brazil uses yellow on black chevrons, as opposed to most of South America, which use black on yellow chevrons.

Roads in Brazil will usually feature double yellow middle lines, with white outer lines. Roads can occasionally have a single dashed yellow line.

It is fairly common to see white painted kerbs on Brazilian roads.

Many houses throughout Brazil have orange tiled roofs.

It is fairly common to see transparent satellite dishes, which within Latin America is generally unique to Brazil.

Blue Fortlev water tanks are often seen on Brazilian roofs.

Car meta: In Generation 3 coverage, you can see two different cars: a white car with a stubby antenna with a diagonal coil wrapped around it, and a white car with no antenna. The car's colour is not always visible. The stubby antenna with a diagonal coil can also be found in Ecuador, Colombia, and Mexico.

The Generation 4 cars you can find in Brazil have a visible front, and have one of three colour patterns: blue, white with a blue stripe, or white.

Brazil also has Generation 4 "smallcam" coverage, mounted lower than regular Generation 4 with a relatively big circular blur with a small protrusion at the front. NOTE: Brazil, Peru and Argentina are the only South American countries with smallcam.

## regional_clues

Most of the coverage in Brazil is along the eastern half, with only sparse coverage in the Amazon.

Area codes are common to see in Brazil. They are often notated using parentheses, or at minimum as two separate numbers. Phone numbers are sometimes without area codes; without them they will be 8 numbers long for landline and 9 for mobile. Notably, area codes ending in 1 will always be around major cities and state capitals.

Each Brazilian state has a two-letter acronym. These show up most prominently in the numbers of state roads (found on kilometre markers).

Postcodes (CEP) are seen frequently on street signs in Brazil. The first two numbers will depict an area, a state or multiple states. The numbers go anti-clockwise from São Paulo, ending in Rio Grande do Sul.

Landscape: The highest mountains are generally found near the southeastern coast.

Northeastern Brazil can be identified by its dry soil, shrubs and succulents (the Caatinga landscape). In rainy season coverage, the shrubs are still recognisable, but can be deceptively green.

The Amazon is a dense rainforest comprising 40% of Brazil's total area. Roads within it mostly go through either biodiverse forest or grassy hills with sparse trees.

Acre has a unique landscape of small grass hills and sparsely growing trees. The roads often do not cut around the hills like in most of Brazil, but ride the hills instead. A similar landscape can also be seen near Maraba in the state of Pará.

Rural locations in Roraima are mostly open grassy plains with large, distant mountains in the background. Occasionally, roads are within the mountains. The grass in Roraima is distinctly thin, and grows in bunches.

The landscape in Rio Grande do Sul can be hilly or flat with lush, green fields. Southern areas can have some resemblance to Uruguay.

Minas Gerais, Rio de Janeiro and Espírito Santo often have rolling grassy hills.

Espírito Santo, and the surrounding areas, often have large mountains with exposed rock.

Distinct trees called Paraná pines have long horizontal branches with leaves only at the end. They grow mainly in the southern Brazilian states of Paraná, Santa Catarina, and Rio Grande do Sul.

Coconut palm trees have long leaves, can grow very tall, and have thin trunks. They are common across the north eastern states.

Carnaúba palms have short but strong, fan-like leaves and a trunk that is smooth at the top and spiked at the bottom. Common in Ceará but also in Piauí and Rio Grande do Norte.

Babassu palms have long, robust leaves comprised of many leaflets. Found throughout the Amazon, or more rarely, down the east coast.

Açai palms produce fruits popular globally. Most found near Belem, but can be seen across the Amazon. Distinct in the way the leaflets droop downward along with their unusually thin trunks.

Moriche palms have leaves with a rounded fan shape. Most common in Amazonas, Acre, and Roraima, but can be found in all of the Amazonian states.

Macaw palms have thin leaves compared to other palms, and often have a visible spear shaped top. Very common around Mato Grosso do Sul and the surrounding states.

A species with long stems and many leaves growing up are very common in Ceará, Rio Grande do Norte, and Paraíba.

Very tall grass can be often seen in the centre of the country, most notably in Tocantins, Goiás, Bahia, and Minas Gerais states.

Agriculture: Eucalyptus Grandis, when fully grown, is a very tall tree with plantations all over Brazil.

Sugarcane plantations are by far most common in the state of São Paulo, and in the coastal north east states, especially Alagoas and Pernambuco.

Soybeans appear as small, leafy soil grown plants. Most commonly grown in Mato Grosso, Paraná, and Goiás.

Coffee beans are by far most common in Minas Gerais, but also in Espírito Santo and northeastern São Paulo.

Infrastructure: Round poles are cylindrical concrete poles often found across the south of Brazil, especially São Paulo. Wooden poles are most commonly seen in Rio Grande do Sul, Roraima, Amazonas, and Rio de Janeiro.

Brazil often uses supports on its electricity poles that connect the crossbar to the stem of the pole. Frequency varies greatly state-to-state. Notably rare in the northeast.

Pole IDs in Goiás are long, yellow pole paint with a black code starting with a number. Bahia and Pernambuco poles use long yellow pole paint with a black code starting with a letter. Paraná poles use black paint with yellow or white painted text. Maranhão poles have a small beige plate with numbers built into the surface (also rarely in Pará and Piauí).

This poletop, with an asymmetrical crossbar and a support reaching to the middle of the longer part of the crossbar, is the most common poletop in São Paulo. Also frequent in Rio Grande do Sul, Rio de Janeiro and Mato Grosso do Sul.

An asymmetrical crossbar with no supports is exclusive to Ceara and Rondonia.

A pole configuration featuring a zigzag-shaped top bar resting atop a flat crossbar is exclusive to Ceara and Roraima.

Pencil poles (smooth cone on top) are common in Rio de Janeiro and rarely in Brasília and Manaus.

Gamma-shaped poles containing electricity boxes at the end of the bar are most commonly seen in Rio de Janeiro and in the cities of Belém, Salvador, Fortaleza and Maceió.

Electricity counters attached to the poles are seen in Cuiabá, Macapá and large cities in the state of Pará.

Poles with one insulator on top and alternating horizontal insulators can be found in Mato Grosso and southern Acre.

Insulators that resemble brown mushrooms are only found in Pernambuco. Insulators that resemble white mushrooms are found in Bahia and rarely Brasília.

Transparent insulators are mostly in Alagoas in the northeast, and Santa Catarina in the south.

Insulators with a shape resembling a spool of thread are found mostly in Rio de Janeiro and Espírito Santo.

Insulators in Ceará can sometimes be uniquely long and thin.

Poles with horizontal insulators are very common in Pará and less so in Mato Grosso.

Long grey insulators are most commonly found in Pará, Mato Grosso, Tocantins, Bahia, Mato Grosso do Sul, Paraná and Santa Catarina.

Black double UFO-shaped insulators in a trident formation are common in Maranhão.

"Lollipop" insulators (spherical or cone shaped suspended on a rod) are very common in Rio Grande de Norte, Paraíba, and Pernambuco.

Dark brown insulators with grey bases are found almost exclusively in Santa Catarina and Rio Grande do Sul.

Brazil often uses metal trash baskets outside houses. In Acre and Rondônia, however, it is much more common to see them made of concrete.

Lamps in Minas Gerais and Goias often feature two parts.

Lamps in urban northern Paraná sometimes have no lamp arm.

Lamps in Roraima have similar styles but different colours in main cities: Boa Vista, Normandia, and Mucajaí have red lamps; Rorainópolis has blue; Pacaraima and Iracema have green; Caracaraí has yellow.

Lamps in Ceará often have a fancy and colourful arm. It sometimes says the town on the arm.

São Paulo has far more divided highways than any other state. Phone numbers can sometimes be seen underneath kilometre markers in São Paulo.

Cobblestone pavement is most common in the northeast and in Rio Grande do Sul.

Roads with hexagonal concrete tiles can be found across Brazil, but more common in Santa Catarina and Minas Gerais.

Some roads in Acre are made of red brick.

Cobblestone roads with randomly sized stones are most common in southeast Rio Grande do Sul and Minas Gerais.

Architecture: Buildings in the southern states, mainly Santa Catarina, can be of European influence (typically vaguely German) in design and style.

Wooden houses on stilts are very common in the state of Acre. Also seen more rarely in low-lying Amazonas and Pará.

Miscellaneous: Most roads in Piauí have red gravel on the side of the road.

In all Generation 4 coverage in Amapá, the front right of the camera is smudged. All Generation 4 coverage in Amapá features the white car with the blue stripe.

You can often see rifts in Alagoas Generation 3 coverage.

Spheres on top of utility poles are João-de-Barro nests. Common across the south of Brazil.

## spotlight

Landscape: The landscape near Uiramuta in northern Roraima is dry, with large mountains containing numerous "folds." Coastal regions in Rio Grande do Sul are recognisable due to being a rare flat wetland.

Infrastructure: São Paulo has a square, yellow pole ID paint. It has three letters on top followed by two numbers, a black line, and four more numbers.

Street signs in Campo Grande and Joinville are stickers wrapped around a pole at intersections. Campo Grande signs usually have extra information and a red and yellow stripe.

Lamps in Rio de Janeiro are curved through the whole of the lamp arm. Teresina lamps are usually blue with an orange arm. Belém lamp heads are often orange. Cuiaba lamps are usually a light green and is one of the few cities that has poles with multiple electricity metres. Lamps in Belo Horizonte and Uberaba in Generation 4 can have a white, numbered plaque attached. Lamps in Caxias do Sul usually have a zig-zag bent pole and a yellow head.

Poles on Santa Catarina Island (Florianópolis) are painted white with blue and red stripes.

Recognisable roads: The 364 through Acre is very recognisable due to the rollercoaster-like landscape and road: the road uniquely rides the hills rather than snaking around them. There is usually no traffic, and is covered mostly by Generation 3 with an antenna.

The Amazonas 319 has a very lush, bright green and tropical landscape, as it cuts through the Amazon rainforest. The road is flat and mostly unpaved, featuring bright orange soil. Closer to Manaus the road becomes paved. There are wooden or ladder poles alongside the entire stretch.

The Pará section of the 163 is often hilly, curvy, dry, and sometimes hazy. It has a wide range of amazonian vegetation. Frequently trafficked by transportation trucks. Covered by the blue Generation 4 car.

Trekkers and unique cars: An Amazonian trekker combines boat and walking coverage on and near the Madeira and Aripuana rivers, south of the 319.

The National Park of Northeast Maranhão, known for its sand dunes with pools of water, has walking trekker and vehicle coverage. The truck is white with a black bed.

Ilha de Fernando de Noronha is approximately 340 kilometres northeast of Rio Grande do Norte. Covered by a white buggy, visible at the front. Has large exposed rocks. There is also boat coverage around the island.
