## general_clues

Ireland has long, white licence plates with the standard European blue strip on the left. The white backplate is especially useful to distinguish it from the UK, which uses yellow backplates (but white fronts). Most UK vehicles also lack the blue strip.

It is very common to see yellow outside dashes on Irish roads. NOTE: This is essentially never found in the UK. These dashes can also be seen in New Zealand.

You will usually find cream white buildings with black roofs in Ireland. Sometimes, the buildings feature grey corner stones. In cities, you will typically find row buildings. NOTE: British style brick buildings can still be found in Ireland.

Ireland is the only European country that uses yellow diamond-shaped warning signs. (This only applies to warning signs. Regulatory signs have the normal red border and white background.)

Irish signage is often bilingual, with English in a regular font, and Irish in italics. Place names, regular words and phrases are all translated. Irish language is somewhat less common on non-official signage.

Irish chevrons are yellow on black. Both single and multiple arrows are common. NOTE: UK chevrons are white on black.

Yellow warning stickers on utility poles distinguish Ireland from UK: UK stickers feature a guy getting zapped by a lightning bolt; Irish stickers usually have a lightning bolt but lack the guy. Irish often have an extra second rectangular sticker with a black outline below the lightning bolt.

Irish yield signs feature the word "YIELD". NOTE: UK yield signs have the phrase "GIVE WAY".

Green and white bollards are a good clue for Ireland. Not entirely unique but especially helpful in 50-50s with the UK.

Rural metal gates with rounded corners are very common in Ireland. NOTE: Useful to distinguish from the UK, where similar gates typically have square corners. Gates in Northern Ireland also typically have rounded corners.

You will often see a strip of grass in the middle of tiny rural roads. Such roads are called "boreens". Often a strong clue in a tough 50-50 between Ireland and the UK.

Similar to UK: Ireland and the UK drive on the left side of the road. The only other European countries to drive on the left are Malta and Cyprus.

Most of Ireland is dominated by a very green, agricultural landscape, with lots of hedgerows next to the roads and dividing the fields. This type of landscape is also common in many parts of the UK.

Both Ireland and the UK use wooden utility poles. Pole steps bolted on the side of the pole are very common — shaped like a series of thin horizontal metal rods.

Double yellow road lines indicate a no parking zone, common in both Ireland and the UK.

Pedestrian crossing signs are somewhat rare in Ireland. They are yellow and diamond-shaped. Instead of pedestrian signs, you will often see black-and-white signposts with a yellow bulb on top — same as the UK.

Ireland has Generation 4 "smallcam" coverage.

## regional_clues

Irish regional roads have 3-digit road numbers. The first digit gives a general region. A good mnemonic: numbers go in an anti-clockwise direction around the island, starting in Dublin. Roads starting with 8 or 9 are newer roads not limited to a region.

Irish phone numbers start with a two digit area code, grouped geographically by first digit.

Ireland's west coast is defined by a rugged, hilly landscape. Steep hills and low mountains dominate. The neat fields of the east make way for wild grass- and moorlands. The grass often has more brownish and yellow tones. Sometimes you will see exposed rocks.

If a very small rural road has Generation 4 coverage and is located in an unusually hilly landscape, you are likely to be in Donegal.

The Wicklow Mountains, just south of Dublin, can look deceptively similar to the western coastal mountains. To distinguish: you will never see the coast from the Wicklow mountains, and they have Generation 4 (instead of Generation 2) on small roads.

An electrical vehicle (EV) with a Generation 4 camera was used to cover most of Eastern Ireland and parts of the Northwest. The back of the EV is visible: grey with a black semicircle. NOTE: Not all Generation 4 coverage in/around Dublin is made with this car.

## spotlight

In the north of counties Sligo and Leitrim you can find distinctive tabletop mountains.

The area in and around Burren National Park is characterised by a unique karst landscape consisting of exposed grey limestone rocks.

Cork is relatively unique in that it is significantly more hilly than the other big Irish cities.
