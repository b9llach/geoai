## general_clues

Spain uses standard long, white European plates with a single blue strip on the left. NOTE: Most Portuguese plates have a yellow strip on the right. Italian plates have two blue strips on either side.

The standard Spanish bollards have a yellow-orange reflector on the front and two white dots on the back. They are typically hollow.

In Spain, you can find a variety of electricity poles and pole tops. In small towns and sparsely populated rural areas concrete ladder poles and wooden poles are the most common. "Ladder" poles have indents that resemble a ladder.

"French style" poletops are common in Spain. They are shaped like an upside down triangle, with a horizontal top bar curved slightly upwards.

High voltage electricity lines supported by pylons are very common in Spain.

Spain has distinct road markers with a variety of colourful borders. The code on top is the number of the road. NOTE: Andorra has similar markers.

In rural areas you will often find hunting signs. Two versions, both unique to Spain. One is a simple white sign with a text starting with "coto" and ending in "caza". The other has a diagonally divided black and white background.

Spain has yellow reflectors on rounded A-type guardrails. NOTE: Belgium, Andorra, Gibraltar and most Latin American countries also have yellow reflectors.

Spanish pedestrian crossing signs have 8 stripes. This is the highest number of stripes in Europe, and almost unique to Spain. NOTE: The only other European country with 8 stripes is Andorra.

Spanish stop signs have a distinct small font. NOTE: Useful to distinguish Portugal from Spain — Portuguese stop signs have a noticeably larger font.

Red borders on road signs extend all the way to the edge of the sign. There is no small white border separating the red part from the edge. NOTE: France, Italy and Portugal have a small white border on the edge.

Almost all traffic signs, kilometre markers, chevrons and some direction signs in Spain have a flat signpost. NOTE: Flat signposts in Europe are also present in Portugal, France and Andorra.

Spain has two chevrons: black with white arrows, and blue with white arrows. NOTE: France is the only other European country that has blue with white chevrons.

Spanish direction signs have a white background. They often feature colourful boxes with road numbers, making them recognizable. Highway signs have a blue background.

Rural roads sometimes have dashed outer lines. This can help tell Spain apart from Portugal and Italy, where such road lines are (almost) non-existent.

Spanish is the main language in most of the country. Some key differences from Portuguese: Ñ is unique to Spanish; Ç and lh are unique to Portuguese; Portuguese uses -ao word ending and -nh- instead of ñ; Spanish uses "de" and "del", while these are often contracted to da, do, das and dos in Portuguese.

The Spanish word for street is "calle".

You will often find no parking signs in front of garage doors and gates. They will usually read "Vado permanente". NOTE: Italy uses signs that read "Passo carrabile".

Spain has highly diverse landscapes, ranging from lush Atlantic forests to bone-dry semideserts.

Generation 3 cars can have long, short or no antennas. The back of the car is usually not visible, but when it is, it can be either black or white. NOTE: Coverage in Eastern Andalusia, Huesca, and Lleida sometimes has a long antenna with a distinctive white top.

Generation 4 cars are blue or less commonly black. The car can be completely hidden.

Spain also has Generation 4 "smallcam" coverage. Spain also has "Shitcam" coverage with a wide circular car blur.

## regional_clues

Spanish regional road numbers feature region-specific codes found on road markers. National roads use codes E, AP, A, R, N. Some autonomous communities only have provincial roads. Asturias uniquely has a large variety of local road codes. Ceuta only has N-roads. Melilla uses the code ML.

Area codes in Spain: every landline number starts with "9", followed by regional numbers. Mobile numbers start with "6".

Regional languages: Catalan is spoken in Catalonia, Valencian Community, and Balearic Islands. Differences from Spanish: "i" instead of "y" for "and"; grave accent (à, è, ò) is exclusive to Catalan within Spain; ç (c with cedilla) is exclusive to Catalan. The Catalan word for street is "carrer". No parking signs in Catalan-speaking areas read "Gual permanent".

Basque is a language isolate spoken in the Basque Country and northern Navarre. Mix of Spanish and Basque signs (often bilingual). Z and K are common letters. Streets in Basque-speaking areas are sometimes called "kalea". Road signs in the Basque Country and Navarre often have distinctive bilingual sub-signs with a thick black border.

Galician is a Romance language closely related to Portuguese, spoken in Galicia. Less dominant than Catalan. Differences from Portuguese: Galician uses ñ and lacks ç. Streets are commonly called Rúa in Galicia. Many Galician town names start with A, O, As or Os articles.

Regional flags of the autonomous communities are reasonably common. The Catalan Independence flag uses the red-and-yellow striped Senyera pattern.

Mainland Spain landscape: highest mountain ranges are the Pyrenees in the northeast, and the Baetic System (including Sierra Nevada) in the southeast. The Meseta Central is a high altitude, mostly flat area in the centre. Surrounded by smaller mountain ranges (Cantabrian Range in north, Iberian System in east, Sierra Morena in south). There are four big river basins: Tajo, Guadiana, Guadalquivir, Ebro.

The landscape in the far north is much more green and lush than the rest of Spain. The landscape is generally quite hilly. Hedges and green meadows are common.

The Pyrenees can be recognized by light grey rock and abundance of pine trees. Local architecture features many rock walls. NOTE: Andorra and the French part of the Pyrenees look very similar.

Thick forests of tall pines: When in smaller mountains in between grazing fields, it will most likely be in the Basque Country or Navarra.

The centre of Castilla y León features a mostly flat agricultural landscape, with cereal crops like wheat and barley. Sunflower plantations are fairly common.

Vineyards in between mountain ranges to the north and south = most likely La Rioja.

Starting in Navarra and cutting across Aragón, there is a strip of semi-desert landscape on both sides of the Ebro river basin.

Very arid looking and mostly flat areas are common in central and south Aragón. Soil is very white and rocky in the northern part. Red soil is more common in Teruel.

Castilla la Mancha can have completely flat landscapes, which are not that common in Castilla y León. Half of Spain's wine is produced in this region.

Open fields sparsely populated with holm oaks (Quercus ilex) and cork oaks (Quercus suber) are very common in Extremadura and neighbouring regions.

Southeast of Spain, especially in Murcia, you can find a rather arid landscape, with very sandy soil and medium sized mountains surrounding.

Mountains in this area, especially Murcia and the south of Alicante have a very distinct rugged look, lacking in vegetation. Hazy look common.

The mountain range of Sierra Nevada features snowy peaks through the months of November and May.

Towns built on a mountain face, consisting entirely of white houses, are very common on the south coast, around Málaga.

Olive trees are one of the main crops planted in Spain — common in more than half of the country. Around Córdoba and Jaén, you can find massive olive tree plantations.

Eucalyptus plantations: in the north coast, especially Galicia. When in dry environment = most likely province of Huelva.

Stone pines (Pinus pinea): forests of exclusively stone pines in flat areas common around Valladolid and Huelva.

Aleppo pines (Pinus halepensis): most common pine on east coast and Balearic Islands.

Baltic pines (Pinus sylvestris): tall and thin, dark bark that sheds at top revealing orange-red wood. Found in the Pyrinees, Sierra de Guadarrama and the Iberian System.

Maritime pines (Pinus Pinaster): two variants in Spain. Plantations of this pine are extremely common in north and central Portugal.

Mainland Spain infrastructure: Most kilometre markers in Spain are either rectangular or square. Some autonomous communities have unique designs (Andalusian markers are especially tall; Madrid markers are square with regional flag at top; Markers in Biscay are bigger with semi-circle shape at top).

Galician towns frequently use signs with a green or blue strip at the top featuring the name of the municipality. "Concello" is the Galician word for municipality.

Most bus stops in Asturias have a red post with the CTA logo at the top. Trash containers in Asturias frequently have the logo of COGERSA. Yellow snow poles are unique to Asturias.

In Aragón, it is common to see a simple flat rectangular white stick bollard with a grey reflector on both sides.

Many signs in Catalonia have round, corrugated signposts. In Catalonia and sometimes the Balearic Islands you will find hunting signs in Catalan reading "Àrea privada de Caça". Within mainland Spain, a square orange reflector bollard is almost exclusive to Lleida and Tarragona in Catalonia.

Big square chevrons with a single arrow are commonly found in Catalonia.

Small square chevrons with a red arrow on a white background are exclusive to Murcia.

Madrid surrounding region has round topped red bus stops, and green buses. In the city centre, you can find urban blue buses.

In Extremadura, normal Spanish bollards with green tops are exclusive to this region.

In Castilla la Mancha you can find stone markers with the region's coat of arms lining up fields.

Poles with a steel mesh structure making rectangular shapes are common in southern Spain, particularly in Murcia and southern Alicante.

Signs from the local government of Andalusia are very recognizable by the green stripe on the left side. Blue and yellow snow poles are unique to Andalusia.

Mainland Spain architecture: Slate roofs (almost black) are found in north-west Spain, ending rather abruptly on the east. Also in Pyrenees near Andorra.

Many houses in Galicia are built with big concrete blocks, sometimes textured to resemble natural stone.

In Galicia, unique granaries called "horreos gallegos" or "cabazos" are very common. In Asturias, "hórreos" are bigger, generally square design, built above four pillars. Slate roofs on horreos = western Asturias.

Large white houses with wooden frames are common in the Basque Country, the Basque part of Navarra, and Cantabria.

Traditional farm houses called "masías" are common in Catalonia, eastern Aragón and parts of the Valencian Community. Usually made of stone.

Within centre and south Spain, towns made completely out of white painted houses are very common in Andalucía and Extremadura.

Mainland Spain miscellaneous: In Asturias you can find a black car with no antenna and a visible brake light.

Special Generation 3 antenna with distinctive white top found in eastern half of Andalusia and in provinces of Huesca and Lleida in northeast.

Balearic Islands: have a recognizable, coastal Mediterranean look. Somewhat dry-looking forests of Aleppo pines cover most of the hills.

Low rock walls are extremely common on the Balearic Islands. The stereotypical Balearic house is painted completely white, with flat roofs or tiled roofs with a small incline.

Within the Balearic Islands: distinctive features differ between Mallorca, Menorca, Ibiza, Formentera. Formentera frequently features low coastal shrubs. The north-west of Palma has a tall mountain range. Menorca has unique features.

Canary Islands: Spanish archipelago off the west coast of Africa. Extremely diverse landscapes including steep mountains, pine forests, volcanic soil, and extreme aridity.

In Lanzarote, very dark, exposed volcanic soil can be seen — superficially resembles the surface of the moon.

Fuerteventura is characterised by a very arid landscape with little to no vegetation. Orange soil very common, within Canary Islands archipelago essentially unique to this island.

A unique bollard design (square orange reflector on front, white reflector on back) is extremely common in the province of Las Palmas (Gran Canaria, Fuerteventura, Lanzarote).

Unique wine plantations with volcanic soil and rocks in semicircle shape are on Lanzarote.

Off the north coast of Lanzarote is La Graciosa — landscape completely barren, entire coverage is overcast.

Ceuta and Melilla: Spanish enclaves in North Africa, partially surrounded by Morocco. Ceuta features north African architecture (flat-roofed houses in warm colours). Almost all Generation 4 coverage in Ceuta features bright blue sky in sunny weather. Mountainous Mediterranean landscape with eucalyptus and pines. Black and white guardrails are unique to Ceuta. Melilla also features similar North African architecture, with hazy look, less saturated sky than Ceuta.

## spotlight

The Camino de Santiago is a network of pilgrim's ways across Europe leading to Santiago de Compostela. Frequently marked with a yellow scallop on a blue background. Common to see pilgrims hiking or biking along these routes during summer.

Coverage during early spring on dirt roads between green grassy fields, and leafless vineyards with red soil, is very common in central-north Castilla la Mancha.

The AP-15 in Navarra features unique bollards with simple flat design and square red reflector. Guardrails also have red reflectors.

Taxis in Barcelona are black with yellow doors, including the trunk door.

The Ebro delta (Deltebre in Catalan) features extremely flat landscape, where rice fields and water canals are very common.

Between Almeria and Motril you can find many greenhouses with white roofs. Especially common in El Ejido.

Bilbao uses unique street signs with elaborately decorated frames.

In Doñana National Park, southwest of Seville, you will find rare and unique car meta.
