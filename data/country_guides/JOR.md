## general_clues

Coverage in Jordan was taken by a black Google car, without a red brake light. NOTE: Jordan is the only Middle Eastern country where a black Google car without an antenna or a red brake light can be found.

Jordanian licence plates are white and can be long or short. Commercial transport vehicles, such as taxis, have a distinctive green strip on the left. NOTE: In the Middle East, the green strip is only found in Jordan. The UAE also uses long white plates. Israel only uses yellow licence plates. In Palestine, both white domestic and yellow Israeli plates are common.

The official language used in Jordan is Arabic, identified by its lines and dots. NOTE: Unlike the UAE, Jordan does not have English writing on most commercial signs.

Roads in Jordan typically feature yellow outside and white middle lines. NOTE: Other Middle Eastern countries such as the UAE and Israel can also have these road lines.

Jordanian chevrons are white with red arrows. NOTE: Israeli chevrons are black with white arrows.

Three common electricity poles in Jordan: rounded concrete poles with diagonal support; rounded concrete poles with three horizontal Y pole tops; thin two-support metal poles with three vertical coils.

Jordan is a very arid country, with limited vegetation and a lot of almost completely lifeless desert. The lushest parts will still only have the look of an arid Mediterranean landscape.

Most buildings in Jordan are made of bright sandstone bricks and have flat roofs. NOTE: Similar sandstone bricks are also common in Jerusalem and the West Bank.

Road quality in Jordan can be poor — well travelled, with heat causing the asphalt to buckle, creating warped cracks. Generally worse than Israel.

## regional_clues

Jordan only has Street View coverage in the far western part of the country, limited to bigger towns and main roads. Coverage ends just north of Jerash. Even Irbid (second largest city) has zero official coverage.

Amman is by far the largest city with extensive coverage. Bustling, developed metropolis with distinct "big city" feel. Slightly hilly. Most of the city consists of medium-sized apartment blocks.

Highway 15 is the longest divided highway with coverage in Jordan. Most of the road is surrounded by desert landscape. Surrounded by rocky hills in the south closer to Aqaba.

Highway 35 connecting Amman and Jerash is a divided road surrounded by hills speckled with green vegetation.

Highway 40 is a recognisable divided road surrounded by low, green shrubbery, west of Amman through hilly terrain into the sandy area close to the Dead Sea.

The Dead Sea can be found directly west along the northern portion of Highway 65. Long, flat, expansive ridge on the other side, useful to differentiate from other bodies of water.

Highway 65 is a single lane, north to south highway. Long, sandy stretches of road south of Feifa valley. Sometimes overcast coverage. Sunset coverage near the airport by Aqaba.

Near and in Madaba, you'll find flat fields, sometimes tilled, with rich, red soil.

This arid rolling hill valley is located near the almost dried out river of Wadi al Hasa on Highway 35. Rounded and smooth rock, single lane highway running through the centre.

For most of Highway 80, divided valley road surrounded by large rigid mountains. East-west road direction.

Long stretch of jagged mountains can be found on the eastern to southern side of Aqaba.

## spotlight

In Amman, taxis are yellow and green.

In Aqaba, taxis are olive green on top and black at the bottom.

In Madaba, taxis are yellow with white bumpers.

In some parts of the town of Wadi Musa, unique mountains with distinctive bulbous rock formations can be seen in the background.

West of Wadi Musa, a collection of trekkers can be found in and around Petra. Rock-cut city from antiquity. Highly eroded reddish rocks, narrow gorges, occasionally groups of tourists. While not likely to appear in competitive maps, these trekkers are a mainstay in many maps including the default World map.
