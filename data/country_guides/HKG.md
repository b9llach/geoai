## general_clues

Hong Kong is located in Southern China, sharing a border with the Guangdong province. It is a Chinese Special Administrative Region, so for country streaks purposes, it is counted as part of China.

Hong Kong has white front licence plates and yellow rear licence plates. The licence plates can be long or short. Some cars have an additional black mainland Chinese licence plate.

Hong Kong drives on the left. NOTE: Singapore also drives on the left, however Taiwan drives on the right.

Hong Kong is a densely populated city with millions of inhabitants. In urban areas, modern skyscrapers are common, with busy streets filled with vehicles and pedestrians. The city has a unique vibe — in many cases, this vibe alone should be sufficient to identify urban Hong Kong locations.

Outside the urban areas, Hong Kong is generally quite mountainous, with green, subtropical vegetation. There is extensive coverage in rural areas outside the city, with many roads going through the mountains. NOTE: Singapore is very flat.

Many extremely tall apartment buildings are found throughout the city. NOTE: Apartment buildings in Singapore and Taiwan tend to be not nearly as tall.

Because of the subtropical climate, air conditioners attached to the walls of apartments are very common in Hong Kong.

Hong Kong has two official languages: English and Chinese (residents primarily speak Cantonese). Bilingual signs are common, with English next to traditional Chinese characters.

Cylindrical grey lamp posts with a wide base are common. Many lamp posts have a white sticker.

Utility poles will never be seen in urban rounds, however you might see cylindrical wooden poles in rural areas.

In Hong Kong, you will almost never see striped kerbs (extremely common in Singapore). Grey pedestrian guardrails are also common.

Hong Kong uses yellow pedestrian crossings. Some intersections have yellow grids.

Hong Kong usually uses white centre lines and white or yellow outer lines. These lines can be single, double or dashed. Roads are commonly made of concrete. Yellow outer lines most commonly indicate no-stopping.

Road signs are white and rectangular, with a thick black outline and bilingual text.

Give way signs feature a white upside down triangle with a thick red border and bilingual text.

Distinct weathered rock walls can be seen on the side of mountains. Some have nets to prevent rockslides. Green or blue plaques are a common sight.

Most fire hydrants are red, usually with circular tops and three protrusions.

Yellow bollards with a white arrow in a blue circle are commonly seen at pedestrian crossings.

Distinct bright orange bins can be found on sidewalks.

Hong Kong taxis come in two variants: sedans or station wagons. Easily identified by silver roofs, green stickers on the front and rear bumper, and the taxi sign on the roof. Red taxis are most common on Hong Kong Island and Kowloon, green taxis in the New Territories, and blue taxis on Lantau Island and Chek Lap Kok.

Buses in Hong Kong are mostly double-decker, with various colour schemes and adverts on the side. Smaller minibuses can also be seen, with a green or red roof. Place names are sometimes visible on the front. NOTE: Double-decker buses are also somewhat common in Singapore but often have a recognizable light green colour scheme.

Boxy houses that are a few storeys high are a common sight in small villages, mostly to the north of Hong Kong.

## regional_clues

A double-decker tram line runs along the north of Hong Kong Island.

The northwestern part of the New Territories is served by a distinct light rail system. Recognised by green-and-orange platforms, single-level train cars and tracks separated from the street.

Mong Kok and surrounding districts in Kowloon: crowded, busy, gridded streets, with apartment blocks situated above the shops. Older buildings, lack of glass walls.

The north-western area near the Mainland China border is mostly flat and features a patchwork of waterlogged rice paddies. The Shenzhen skyline is visible in the distance, to your north.

The Central District: large concentration of modern skyscrapers. Feels quite busy, affluent and well-kept, with lots of luxury brand stores and ads.

Two skyscrapers can determine whether you are on Kowloon or Hong Kong Island side: IFC is on Hong Kong Island, ICC on Kowloon. ICC has straighter edges (cuboid-like), IFC is rounder and has noticeable vertical window frames.

Mid-Levels is a residential district recognized by non-identical tall modern apartments and hilliness.

Sai Wan Ho residential district has a lot of identical white and brown apartment blocks, and tall glass office buildings.

Chek Lap Kok Island and the Boundary Crossing Facilities have many large scale construction works. Identified by many bridges connecting them to other parts of Hong Kong.

Caribbean Coast and Tung Chung residential district has identical apartment blocks facing the same direction, like a "wall" of buildings. Lots of planted palms.

## spotlight

The Tsing Ma Bridge is the only major suspension bridge in Hong Kong, with two ladder-like support towers, and yellow and red guardrails.

Ting Kau Bridge: cable-stayed, generally clean asphalt, three cylindrical support towers, hangers coming directly from towers, yellow paint in median and outsides.

Stonecutters Bridge: similar to Ting Kau but lacks yellow paint, has box beam guardrail design.

Lung Wo Road: Hong Kong Island skyline to the south and Kowloon skyline to the north. Good condition, clean sidewalks.

Tai Chung Kiu Road: divided road running northeast, views of Shing Mun River to the west, palm trees in median.

Fan Kam Road: dense tropical vegetation, sometimes mountains in background. Narrow, no outer lines. Many small villages branch off.

Lam Kam Road: similar to Fan Kam but more east-west. Busier, more cars, more houses on sides.

The Kai Tak Cruise Terminal: shiny, cylindrical appearance, with triangular windows along the side.

The Hong Kong-Zhuhai-Macao Bridge can be seen from South Perimeter Road, on Chek Lap Kok Island. Its length stretches into the horizon, spanning across the ocean.

The roads going to Disneyland are divided, with palm trees and teal coloured street lamps.

Between Hong Kong Island and Lantau Island, three unique islands have trekker coverage: Peng Chau, Cheung Chau and Lamma Island. Lack of cars, narrow streets, rural architecture. Lamma Island is the hilliest.

The Lugard Road trekker around Victoria Peak: distinct black guardrail, view of skyline, tourists walking along the path.
