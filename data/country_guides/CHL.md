## general_clues

Chilean road lines are either all white or all yellow. All white is most common. Within the Americas, all white is a good clue for Chile, though not completely unique. All yellow road lines are seen in National Parks or areas of high snowfall — within the Americas, these are essentially unique to Chile. You will almost never see a combination of the two colours, apart from a few rare exceptions. NOTE: Argentina has a decent amount of roads with all white road lines.

Chile has a solid white Google Car with no antenna in Generation 3. Two variants: a plain white car, and a white car with a visible black section (unique to Chile). A blue Google Car can be seen in Generation 4. NOTE: In Generation 3, a plain white Google Car with no antenna can also be seen in Peru and Bolivia.

Chile uses square concrete poles with an indent on both sides. Somewhat similar to Brazilian poles but with fewer horizontal supports in the indent. The upper part typically has small pinholes. Wooden poles are also fairly common.

Regular vehicles have white licence plates, while commercial and taxi vehicles have orange plates. NOTE: Orange plates can also be seen in Ecuador. Brazilian commercial plates are red.

Slots can often be seen at the top of signposts.

Chilean bollards are similar to Spanish style bollards, with a white reflector at the front and a yellow-orange reflector at the back. They are not super common.

You may sometimes find an orange, temporary bollard with two white stripes at the top.

Bridges in Chile often have red painted fence posts.

## regional_clues

Chile is a long and narrow country that spans more than 4,000 kilometres from north to south, meaning that its climate varies significantly due to latitude. The northern parts are dry and arid (mild desert). In the centre, around the capital, the climate becomes temperate Mediterranean. In the southernmost regions, the climate is cold and wet (cool oceanic).

Chile uses a memorable regional road naming system, where the first letter increases between A and Y from north to south.

Chilean phone codes will sometimes have a two digit area code in front, followed by seven digits. Most central states also have different area codes for specific provinces.

The Atacama is an extremely arid desert covering the northern third of Chile. Some parts experience the lowest rainfall on the planet. Many locations are almost completely devoid of vegetation. NOTE: The Atacama Desert also covers parts of southern Peru, western Bolivia and northwestern Argentina.

The region of Arica y Parinacota can be recognized by smooth, sandy mountains that may look slightly eroded, or have bulbous formations. Valleys are also common.

Rocky, uneven mountains are most commonly seen in Atacama and northern Coquimbo.

Brownish red soil can sometimes be seen in Atacama region. Most reliable in Generation 3.

Eucalyptus plantations combined with a somewhat dry climate are a good indicator that you are in southwestern Valparaíso.

The region around Santiago can be recognized by its semi-arid climate, having dry hills and scattered vegetation.

Compared to Santiago, the Maule region has a similar look but has a Mediterranean climate and is generally greener. Vineyards are also commonly seen.

Concepcion pines (Pinus radiata) recognized by their puffy needles are most commonly found in Bio Bio, less commonly in Ñuble and Araucania.

Narrow roads with trees on both sides can commonly be seen in Bio Bio and Araucania.

Monkey puzzles (Araucaria araucana) are found in far eastern Araucania. Also found on the other side of the Argentinian border.

A rolling, agricultural landscape comparable to New Zealand can be seen on Isla Grande de Chiloé.

Chilean rhubarb can commonly be found on Isla Grande and on the mainland east of it. Recognized by its large, cabbage-like appearance.

Lush green valleys with rocky mountains and often overcast coverage can be seen in southern Los Lagos and northern Aysén.

Southern Aysén can be recognised by autumn coverage, having orange and red coloured vegetation.

## spotlight

The A-31 can be recognised by its high altitude, with a steep drop into a valley to the north. Has Gen 3 sunset coverage.

Generation 4 sunset coverage can be seen on Ruta 21 from Calama to San Francisco de Chiu Chiu. In the west near Calama, the sun has just started to set, causing a giant white dome in the sky. In the east, the sky has a warm gradient.

Ruta 1 is a coastal road easily recognized by the wall of mountains to the east, and the Pacific Ocean to the west.

Part of Ruta 41 is located in a valley with snowy mountains in the distance. Many trees and vineyards toward the west. Triple yellow lines can also be seen.

Purple Large-leaved Lupines flowers can commonly be found along Ruta 240.

All yellow lines on a concrete road indicate a paved road in the Magallanes region (very south of Chile). Landscape tends to be barren, grassy, and treeless. NOTE: Ruta 9 only appears in Generation 3, whereas the other roads only appear in Generation 4 coverage.

A distinct landscape consisting of a line of snow-capped mountains with two other isolated, symmetrical snow-capped volcanoes are seen in Lauca National Park.

The northern section of Pampa del Tamarugal National Reserve can be recognized by Tamarugo trees lining the road.

Alto Loa National Reserve can be recognized by the vast, open landscape, followed by salt pans leading up to snow-capped volcanoes.

Smooth mountains with a reddish tint are seen in Los Flamencos National Reserve. Salt pans are also a common sight.

Nevado Tres Cruces National Park: vast, open landscape, salt pans, line of mountains, sand has reddish tint. The east has the Google car driving up a mountain pass with views of lightly snow-capped volcanoes and snow on the side of the road.

Llanos de Challe National Park: brown coloured dirt on roadside, patches of light sand, juniper plants, and dark coloured mountains. Sky overcast in most coverage.

Alto Bio Bio National Reserve: vast, mostly treeless landscape combined with triple yellow lines. Distinct cold and high altitude feel. Monkey puzzles toward the east.

Puyehue National Park: dense vegetation surrounding the road. Pavement has triple yellow lines, tar snakes frequently seen. In the east, many dead trees can be seen with a lack of leaves.

Cities: A slight, sandy ridge with a gradual incline can be seen in Arica. A larger, towering ridge can be seen in Iquique. A rockier and more uneven ridge can be seen in Antofagasta.

San Pedro de Atacama can be recognized by its clay brick walls, commonly with triangles at the top.

Orange street signs are at almost every intersection in Lanco, occasionally in nearby Malalhue.

Villa O'Higgins can be recognized by the large mountains immediately to the east. Snow-capped mountains seen in the distance to the west. Street signs and fences made of orange-stained wood.

Lamps: Orange lamps most often seen in Calama and Chañaral, and rarely Antofagasta. Lamps that look like green domes seen in Copiapo. Lamps in Santiago very commonly have photocells. Grey lamps with thin flat top and rounded underside found in Talca. Blue rectangular lamps with yellow stripe in Concepción. Lamps with silver top and grey bottom found in Puerto Montt and Temuco.

Poles: Poles in Arica feature white paint with a black number, while other Atacama Desert cities feature poles with yellow paint. Round poles with black paint can be seen in El Salvador.

Special Google cars: Google car towing a white and silver "Outback" trailer south of the Chilean National Zoo in Santiago. A grey Toyota Hilux with various gear and a snorkel on the front right can be seen from around Baños Morales. Conguillio National Park has a white truck with visible side mirrors and antenna on front right. Puerto Williams (southernmost town covered) has a white pickup truck with visible side mirrors.

Trekkers: Atacama Large Millimetre Array recognized by many radio telescopes in a barren, snowy environment. ALMA OSF has large radio telescopes and rural facilities. Horse trekker northeast of San Felipe (similar trekker on Argentinian side). Torres del Paine area: sharp peaked mountains, trekker walking on a glacier with other people in orange helmets.
