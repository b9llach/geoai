## general_clues

Indonesian licence plates are black with white letters. The letters usually appear as three white sections through the blur. Commercial vehicles use yellow licence plates. Plates in newer coverage can be white too. NOTE: Malaysian licence plates are also black, but generally have two white sections.

Indonesia drives on the left. NOTE: Malaysia drives on the left, while the Philippines drives on the right.

Indonesia uses white or yellow centre lines. Outer lines will always be white. The centre lines will be solid or dashed. In Generation 3 coverage, yellow centre lines are more rare. NOTE: Malaysian road lines are always all white.

Poles in Indonesia are typically round and made out of either steel or concrete. Many of the steel poles are painted black, and also feature the Indonesian flag colours on them. The pole tops are usually either even or uneven, depending on the region.

Indonesia commonly uses black-and-white square or circular bollards, as well as yellow-and-black ones (which can also have a red reflector). NOTE: UK-style bollards are mostly found in Kalimantan.

Yellow waystones angled towards the road, with a cutout close to the top, are unique to Indonesia. They include shorthand for three cities or towns. NOTE: Waystones in the Philippines are perpendicular to the road and have text facing away from the road.

Indonesian can look very similar to Malaysian. Indonesian was influenced by Dutch, Malaysian by English. Example: 'television' = 'televisi' in Indonesian (similar to Dutch 'televisie'), 'televisyen' in Malaysian (similar to English).

Rows of red and white banners can be found all around Indonesia. Sometimes more colourful flags can be found.

Cigarette ads can be found all over Indonesia, usually with a black or red colour scheme and a price around 18,000 Rp. Look for graphic pictures and the word 'PERINGATAN'.

Motorcycles in Indonesia have front licence plates, as opposed to other south-east Asian countries.

The official Indonesian currency is the Rupiah (Rp).

## regional_clues

Coverage in Indonesia is fairly limited. No coverage on Papua or adjacent islands; sparse coverage in Aceh, Central Sulawesi, North Maluku.

Names of 2nd level subdivisions ('Kabupaten' or 'Kab' for short, meaning regency) can be found on signs and billboards. Each province is split into multiple Kabupatens, totalling 417 covered.

Cardinal directions on addresses: North = Utara; East = Timur; South = Selatan; West = Barat; Central = Tengah.

Infrastructure: Indonesia uses two types of pole tops, even and uneven. The bar that holds the insulators is symmetrical (even) or not (uneven) to the pole.

Poles with a triangle attachment under the horizontal bar are found in specific regions.

Poles with an indent on both sides are found in West Java, Jakarta, and Banten.

Trident style pole tops are most commonly found in Central Java and Yogyakarta, rarely in South and West Kalimantan, and North Sumatra and Riau.

Bali features both even and uneven poles, plus a unique rectangle attachment on pole tops.

On the Lesser Sunda Islands ("Nusas"), poles have an extra long support on the pole top.

Pole tops in North and Central Sulawesi, and Gorontalo commonly feature an extra attachment right under the horizontal bar.

Thin, black poles next to regular poles near the East Peninsula of Central Sulawesi often have two pieces of tape near the top, usually light blue and green.

Poles with even bar but uneven insulators (one on one side, two on the other) are almost exclusive to North Sumatra.

Satellite dishes can help determine if you are north or south of the equator (the dish points away from your hemisphere).

Central Sulawesi features unique bollards painted black on bottom, white in middle, yellow on top.

Painted fences (mostly white with blue or red) are typically found on Sulawesi.

Green paint can often be seen on the side of bridges in South and Central Kalimantan.

Landscape: Within Indonesia, oil palm plantations are mostly concentrated in Sumatra and Kalimantan.

The majority of rice production is concentrated in Java, with some in South Sulawesi and South Sumatra.

The Nusas typically look drier than other regions. Especially dry towards the east, in Generation 3. Vegetation tends to look brown with lack of leaves. Sumba Island is the driest of the Nusas.

A savannah landscape is occasionally seen on North Sumba.

Red soil can often be found on Batam and the Bangka Belitung islands, located east of mainland Sumatra.

Architecture: Tiled roofs are most common on Java, sometimes nearby regions (southern Sumatra, Lesser Sunda Islands, South Kalimantan). Tiles in South Kalimantan and Jambi look slightly different — flatter.

Plain sheet metal roofs are more common toward the north (Kalimantan, Sulawesi, Sumatra).

Horizontal slots inside the triangles of roofs are almost unique to Southern Sulawesi.

Distinct roofs with uniquely high-pitched peaks are found on Sumba.

Architecture in Bali was influenced by old Balinese culture and Hinduism. Crown-like roof pinnacles, guardian statues, slit-drum towers, and andesite (dark volcanic rock) are common.

West Sumatra features distinct boat-shaped roofs.

North Sumatra features similar boat-shaped roofs, however less angled upward and without spires. Most commonly found near Lake Toba.

On Kalimantan, you can find unique roofs made out of wooden chips.

Roofs with gable horns are most common in South Sulawesi, Riau, and Central and South Kalimantan.

A small region of South Sulawesi around Rante Pao features unique houses called Tongkonan, with saddle-shaped roofs. Houses elevated from the ground.

Miscellaneous: Regional licence plate codes — 1 letter codes are only seen in Java (mostly west). 2 letter codes still seen on Java but in the east.

Postal codes are five digits long, grouped by starting digit (1xxxx in Jakarta to 9xxxx in Maluku and Papua).

Indonesia is religiously a Muslim majority country, so seeing churches and other Christian signs is a good clue for specific regions.

Generation 3 antenna coverage is most commonly found on Java, very rarely on Bali in Denpasar area. Most common in East Java.

Black Generation 3 coverage can be found almost everywhere in Indonesia, but is most common in Sulawesi.

## spotlight

Lake Toba region in North Sumatra: mountains surrounding the lake and Samosir Island in the middle. Overcast coverage common.

In some Generation 4 coverage in Batam, a purple tint and a black blurred object can be seen. Most common in Batam, sometimes spotted in nearby regions (North Sumatra, around Medan).

Palu Bay is a unique slightly north-west facing bay just north of Palu city, with mountains to the west and a flat, more urbanised landscape to the east.

Steeply pitched roofs made of palm tree leaves can sometimes be seen on Nias Island, southwest of North Sumatra.

A white SUV with black bars and a wooden plank on the roof can rarely be seen in eastern Bali.

A black cab with men sitting in the truck bed can be seen near Jagasatru on Bali.
