## general_clues

The English-speaking parts of Canada use British English spelling (e.g. centre; colour). Canada mostly uses the metric system. For example, speed is measured in kilometres per hour. NOTE: This is useful to distinguish between the US, where American English spelling and (usually) the imperial system are used.

Canada has a fair amount of Generation 2 coverage, especially in rural or remote areas. Generation 2 can be recognized by the big circular car blur and the halo-like colour distortion in the sky. NOTE: Generation 2 is very rare in the US. If you are between Canada and the US, and the location is in Generation 2, you are much more likely to be in Canada.

Generation 3 is significantly more common in Canada than in the US. The side mirrors are blurred out differently: while they generally appear as two peaks in Canadian Gen 3 coverage, you will mostly see a single peak in the US.

Canada also has Generation 4 "smallcam" coverage, mounted lower than regular Generation 4 with a relatively big circular blur with a small protrusion at the front.

Canada uses unique diamond-shaped T-Intersection signs. Some provinces use different designs. NOTE: Canada is the only country in the world where you can find these signs. The US uses rectangular T-Intersection signs.

Canada uses the word 'maximum' on speed signs. Because speed is measured in kilometres instead of miles, the numbers tend to be higher than the US. NOTE: The US uses the words 'speed limit' on such signs. Speed limits ending with 5 are more common in the US.

Canadian roads usually have single yellow centre lines. However, double middle yellow centre lines are still decently common. NOTE: Double yellow centre lines are more common in the US.

You can sometimes find communal mailboxes in Canada. Personal mailboxes are still very common. Canada Post has a red logo. NOTE: These communal mailboxes are very rare in the US. The US Postal Service has a blue logo.

Canadian road signs tend to be less wordy compared to their US counterparts. For railroad crossing and yield signs: in the US, these usually have the literal words 'railroad crossing' and 'yield' written on them, whereas in Canada, these signs are typically blank.

## regional_clues

Each province has its own unique licence plates, some recognizable from afar even when blurred. Multiple provinces do not require front plates.

French is the majority language in Québec and parts of New Brunswick. NOTE: At the federal level, French has official status alongside English, so you can sometimes find bilingual signs in other parts of Canada.

Each province has their own highway shields with unique designs. The green Trans-Canada Highway shield can be found in all 10 provinces.

Each province has its own unique kilometre markers. Notably, Ontario KM markers have a white square at the bottom, which is unique to the province.

Some provinces have unique speed signs. Ontario speed limit signs may have a black section on the bottom with the words 'km/h' or 'begins'. British Columbian signs include 'km/h' but lack the black section. Québec can sometimes have French on speed limit signs.

Alberta has a unique black and white bollard found at intersections. It has a cylindrical shape and two black bands that wrap all the way around. Some Alberta bollards have a yellow band between the two black bands instead of white. Blue bands can rarely be seen in and around Calgary; green bands rarely in and around Edmonton.

Manitoba uses black and grey painted bollards. Most of the time an orange stripe will be present below the grey one. Wooden, rectangular bollards are typically found further north in Manitoba.

British Columbia has black-and-white bollards with a thin, slightly curved shape. Most commonly found on Vancouver Island.

Québec has unique bollards always attached to guardrails. They are long, thin white poles with a red or green reflector at the top.

Yellow fire hydrant markers atop signposts are found in Québec. In Québec City these markers will usually have a blue rectangle and markers in Trois-Rivières are distinctly small.

Rectangular markers on the sides of roads are found in Québec.

Diamond-shaped markers on the sides of roads are found in Ontario.

In Ontario, you will often see guardrails consisting of cylindrical wooden posts, held together by precisely three metal wires. Some posts are marked by a reflective white band.

Flat, yellow bollards can be found on the Trans-Canada highway in New Brunswick.

The Northwest Territories has a black and white bollard with a cylindrical shape and two thick, black bands. NOTE: Looks similar to Alberta but Alberta bollards have thinner black bands.

Transformers on poles will face the road in British Columbia and New Brunswick, while in other provinces, they will be parallel with the wires. Transformers on Prince Edward Island and Nova Scotia will be installed at a 45° angle relative to the road. On PEI, red numbers will usually be written on the side.

Distinctive pole tops with two side insulators and one on top are most commonly found in Ontario.

Some Québec poles have recognizable pole tops with insulator rods that are angled slightly upwards.

Québec poles may sometimes have a vertical black and yellow label with a small 'Q' in the black part.

Poles on Prince Edward Island usually feature a small white sticker.

Pole top insulators in Saskatchewan alternate.

Québec T-Intersection signs do not feature the chequerboard pattern. Newfoundland T-Intersection signs feature a rectangular yellow border around the diamond shape.

Road signs in British Columbia can have green backsides. Green backsides are also somewhat common on Prince Edward Island.

Signs in British Columbia are sometimes hung down from unique signposts. British Columbia predominantly uses concrete barriers instead of guardrails.

Stop signs with the word 'Arrêt' are unique to Québec. Stop signs with both 'Stop' and 'Arrêt' are most commonly found in New Brunswick.

Bilingual street signs in New Brunswick. Bilingual street signs are also found in other French minority areas of Canada.

Rural roads in Alberta are numbered sequentially, making it easy to figure out your exact location using just a road sign. Township roads (Twp Rd) run east to west, increasing the more north. Range roads (Rge Rd) run north to south, increasing the more west.

Rural roads in Manitoba are organised in a grid and referred to only by their number and cardinal direction.

In Newfoundland and Labrador, most wooden posts on guardrails have a pyramid shaped top.

Fire hydrants in Newfoundland often have a diamond-shaped marker next to them.

Wooden boxes holding up signs can be spotted in Newfoundland and Labrador.

Within Eastern Canada, double yellow lines are most common in Nova Scotia, followed by Québec and Prince Edward Island. Ontario prefers single yellow lines.

Ontario uses distinct orange and black striped traffic cones. Québec has unique orange and white striped traffic cones.

Québec is the only Canadian province that uses red and white chevrons.

New Brunswick and Nova Scotia commonly use lampheads with a rectangular shape.

Lamp arms on utility poles in Nova Scotia are usually curved and much shorter than in the rest of Canada.

Landscape: Western Canada is very mountainous. The highest mountains are in British Columbia, western Alberta, and parts of Yukon.

Yukon has a very northern, boreal landscape that is mostly hilly, sometimes with large mountains in the distance. Coniferous trees line the roads. Older Generation 2 coverage is still very common.

The landscape in the interior of southern British Columbia, mostly centred around Kamloops and Kelowna, is generally hilly and a little more arid than the rest of BC. Vegetation consists of sprawling brown grasslands and pine tree forests.

The Great Plains are mostly flat, with some rolling hills. Lots of agriculture, very few trees, and very sparsely populated. The driest parts are near the border with Montana.

Canola is grown in the central southern prairies, almost nowhere else in Canada. Visually similar to a field of bright yellow flowers.

The region around Rouyn-Noranda in northwestern Québec has a north-south grid with a slightly hilly landscape, agricultural fields, and forests with shorter trees and thin birches.

Southern Saskatchewan has many river valleys, giving it a barren hilly landscape much more so than the rest of the Canadian Great Plains.

Aspen trees are common in the forested parts of Manitoba, Saskatchewan and Alberta. Recognizable by their white, birch-like trunks. In fall coverage, they turn a characteristic bright yellow.

The Canadian Shield extends through eastern Canada. You will commonly see exposed rocks.

The Québec City-Windsor Corridor is the most densely populated part of Canada. Mostly flat and green, with a patchwork of agriculture and occasional forest cover.

Prince Edward Island soil has a distinct red tint. Potato farming is also very common on PEI.

The Balsam Fir is a very common species of tree found in Newfoundland. Christmas tree-like appearance.

Canadian phone numbers start with a three-digit area code formatted XXX XXX XXXX.

Provincial flags appear decently often. The Fleurdelisé of Québec in particular is very common.

Bins made of wood planks can be seen on Newfoundland driveways. Most commonly octagonal.

Acadia is a French-speaking cultural region in the Canadian Maritimes. It has its own flag — a French-style tricolour with a yellow star in the blue band.

## spotlight

The Dempster Highway connects the Klondike Highway in Yukon with the remote town of Inuvik. Unpaved with Generation 2 coverage, dark brown soil, tundra landscape with short trees. Closer to Inuvik the weather gets cloudy.

The Top of the World Highway west of Dawson City in Yukon has overcast Generation 2 coverage and is located mostly high up in the mountains.

A group of motorcyclists can be seen in front of the Google car on Highway 1 near Beaver Creek in Yukon.

The AB-93 highway through Banff and Jasper National Park features distinct, gradual, and steep mountains with sharp peaks on either side.

The area near Drumheller, Alberta has distinct canyon-like treeless rock formations.

QC-167 and QC-113 are covered in Gen 3 and identifiable for their overcast autumn coverage, often with snow on the ground.

The inland roads of the Gaspé Peninsula feature densely forested mountainous terrain. Common to encounter overcast coverage.

The Cape Breton Highlands in northeastern Nova Scotia have an unusual amount of elevation for Eastern Canadian standards.

Route 430 on Newfoundland runs in a roughly south-southwest to north-northeast direction with washed out road lines, in a remote and sparsely populated area.

Iqaluit is the capital of Nunavut. The city features bilingual turquoise street signs.

The remote town of Churchill in northern Manitoba features a trekker with a distinct thin antenna visible on the front right side of the car.

Tulita, NWT has a unique Google car and dark-coloured soil.

Sanikiluaq is a remote town situated in Hudson Bay. Snowy town covered by a snowmobile.

Inukjuak is a town in northern Québec recognized by the snowmobile visible through the blur.

Grise Fiord is the northernmost covered town in Canada. Recognized by unique mountains and ice floes on the coast.

Resolute can be recognized by the barren landscape with visible patches of snow.

Cambridge Bay is the only Nunavut settlement to have Generation 2 trekker coverage.

Qikiqtarjuaq features distinct mountains and red tinted soil.

Kangiqsualujjuaq is a town in northern Québec, surrounded by green rocky hills, with trees visible in the distance.

Nain is a small town on the Northeastern coast of Labrador with colourful wooden houses.

Red Bay is a town on the southern coast of Labrador, slightly red soil, most houses painted white.

The northernmost Google Street View coverage in the world is a trekker on Ellesmere Island in northern Nunavut. Shows a glacier in a rocky, desolate landscape.
