## general_clues

UK front plates are white, while back plates are yellow. Some plates have a blue strip on the left, but most do not. Additionally, you might see a plate with a green 'Zero Emissions' strip. NOTE: Ireland uses white front and back plates, always with a blue strip on the left.

The UK drives on the left side of the road. NOTE: The UK, Ireland, Malta and Cyprus are the only European countries that drive on the left.

Double yellow road lines are often found on the sides of marked roads, especially in urban areas. These lines indicate a no stopping zone. NOTE: These lines can also be found in Ireland.

Middle lines that consist of long dashes with cat's eyes (reflectors) in the gaps between the dashes are a good clue for the UK.

The most common bollard in the UK has a rounded shape, with a red reflector on the front and a white one on the back. The base and top are black, with a white stripe in the middle.

The UK uses wooden poles. Pole steps bolted on the side of the pole are very common, shaped like a series of thin horizontal metal rods. NOTE: Ireland also commonly uses wooden poles with pole steps.

Chevrons are black with white arrows. NOTE: Irish chevrons are black with yellow arrows. French chevrons are blue with white arrows.

The UK uses triangular warning signs with a red border and a white background. NOTE: Useful to distinguish UK from Ireland — Ireland uses diamond-shaped yellow warning signs.

Direction signs in the UK can be blue, green, or white. Look for the letters A or M, as these are the major roads. Smaller roads start with B. NOTE: Irish direction signs are typically bilingual, with Irish names in italics.

The UK commonly uses white street signs with black text, positioned low to the ground. Usually supported by two small posts. Many street signs in suburban areas have a small cul-de-sac icon. They can sometimes have different colours or feature small designs like coats of arms.

UK yield signs feature the phrase "GIVE WAY". NOTE: Irish yield signs just have the word "YIELD".

The yellow warning stickers on utility poles can be a good way to distinguish UK from Ireland: UK stickers feature a guy getting zapped by a lightning bolt; Irish stickers usually have a lightning bolt but lack the guy getting zapped.

A yellow traffic bollard is commonly found in the UK.

Pedestrian crossing signs are almost non-existent in the UK. Instead, pedestrian crossings will often have black-and-white striped poles with a yellow light bulb on top.

Rural metal gates with square corners are very common in the UK. NOTE: Useful to distinguish from Ireland, where similar gates typically have rounded corners. Gates in Northern Ireland also typically have rounded corners.

The British countryside tends to be very green, with lots of hedgerows lining the road. Low, rolling hills are common.

Terraced houses (row houses) are very common in the UK. This housing type shares walls on both sides with neighbouring homes. You will also see a lot of semi-detached houses. NOTE: 61% of the population lives in terraced or semi-detached houses, significantly more than most other European countries except Ireland and the Netherlands.

Red brick buildings are common in many parts of the UK. NOTE: Red brick architecture is also common in the Netherlands, Belgium, and Denmark.

UK suburbs typically feature near-identical houses, often semi-detached, usually with well-manicured lawns. The road layout can be maze-like, with lots of cul-de-sacs and curvy roads.

Many British buildings have distinct-looking chimneys with multiple chimney pots. These pots are usually round and brick-coloured.

Some buildings, both old and new, feature black-and-white timber framing. NOTE: Less common in Ireland.

The UK has Generation 4 "smallcam" coverage.

## regional_clues

Occasionally, you can find one of the 4 country flags (England, Scotland, Wales, Northern Ireland).

A- and B-roads are grouped by their first digit, for example A380 and B3217 are in the southwest.

Landline phone numbers in the UK always have nine or ten digits after the 0 trunk code. Notable codes: all of Northern Ireland uses 028; all of London uses 020.

Bins in urban areas often have the city name on them.

The Scottish Highlands can be distinguished by the semi-barren, very mountainous landscape.

In Scotland, you can often find red and white bollards, round and bulky, with a red-and-grey reflector that wraps all the way around. Sometimes found in other parts of the UK. NOTE: Looks similar to French bollard, but Scottish is round on top while French is more spiked.

Diamond-shaped 'Passing Place' signs are most commonly found in Scotland.

Welsh is a Celtic language spoken throughout Wales. Road signs in Wales are usually bilingual English and Welsh. Welsh uses D, W, Y much more commonly than English. The double LL is also very distinct. NOTE: Scottish Gaelic and Irish are both also Celtic languages.

In Wales, markings on the road are in both English and Welsh. 'Araf' means 'Slow' and is only found in Wales.

Scottish Gaelic is a Celtic language closely related to Irish. Common letter clusters: bh, dh, fh, mh, ai, ea, eo, ui. Found most commonly in the Scottish Highlands, almost always north of Glasgow. NOTE: Scottish Gaelic on road signs will often be in green or yellow text.

Houses made of large blocks of sandstone or granite are common in Scotland. Granite buildings are common in Aberdeen; red sandstone in Glasgow and Dumfries; yellow sandstone in Edinburgh.

Cornish palms (Cordyline australis) are most commonly found in the far southwest along the coast and in Jersey.

Black stickers with yellow text sometimes appear on lampposts in Northern Ireland.

The Isle of Man, Jersey, Gibraltar, Bermuda, Falkland Islands, South Georgia, Pitcairn Islands, and British Indian Ocean Territory are all British Overseas Territories or Crown Dependencies counted as part of the UK for country streaks.

## spotlight

The Lake District is very hilly, with a lot of exposed rocks and stone walls. It can look like the Scottish Highlands, but Scotland is generally less rocky.

London street signs have a distinct look. They usually feature the street name in black, followed by the postcode and borough in red.

Street name signs in Aberdeen and Aberdeenshire typically feature a simple blue background with white lettering. NOTE: Central Aberdeen uses a different black-and-yellow design.

Signs with green text underneath the street name, indicating the townland, can be found in the western part of Northern Ireland.

Most street signs in the Wakefield council are black with a white outline and letters, using the Monotype Rockwell Nova font.

Some Generation 4 coverage in London was taken by a white electric vehicle, with a distinct back of the car visible. NOTE: A similar car was used in Dublin and Germany.

The majority of poles and signposts in Birmingham have either yellow tape or yellow paint wrapped around them.

Speed signs with green borders with the text "Twenty's Plenty" are mostly found in and around Glasgow.
