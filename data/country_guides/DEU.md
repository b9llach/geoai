## general_clues

All car coverage in Germany was taken with a Generation 4 camera. NOTE: There are some Generation 2 and 3 trekkers, and spill coverage with older dates.

Germany uses black and white bollards, with white and light-grey reflectors. The reflector will be orange on bollards next to intersections. They sometimes have plates containing the road number. Many bollards also feature blue attachments on the side. NOTE: Many European countries have similar bollards, however most will have different coloured reflectors. Luxembourg uses almost identical bollards, but they have 3 bolts instead of 2 on the reflector.

The official language of Germany is German, which uses the special letters Ä, Ö, Ü and ß. NOTE: The other majority German-speaking countries with coverage are Switzerland, Austria and Liechtenstein. German is also spoken in East Belgium and the Italian province of South Tyrol.

Most wooden poles in Germany will feature a rectangular white sticker.

Germany has a wide variety of different poles and pole tops. The most common pole tops being the horizontal bar with uneven hanging insulators, Triangle pole top, and all kinds of T-Shaped pole tops.

Germany has a wide variety of sign backs. Notably, you can commonly find black or dark grey sign backs, which are rather rare in Europe. Most signs will also feature a sticker with an upside-down red triangle on a black rectangle. NOTE: Italy, Romania and Albania also have black sign backs.

Like Austria, Germany uses a wide variety of regional bus stops, all of which have the big yellow and green H symbol on them.

Mailboxes in Germany are bright yellow and feature a black logo.

You will find an excessive amount of bollards on most roads in Germany — around every 50 metres.

German town entry signs are yellow with black text and have a metallic frame. They can include the town/city name, the Municipality, and the county.

Germany uses blue rectangular kilometre markers with a white border for A-Roads (Autobahn). B-Roads and local roads have white markers with a black border.

German pedestrian signs have five stripes and the person has a belt. NOTE: The same design is used in Luxembourg.

One-way traffic signs with the word 'Einbahnstraße' written on them are unique to Germany. NOTE: In Austria, there will be "EINBAHN" written on the sign.

"No Stopping" and "No Waiting" signs in Germany will often have white arrows drawn on them.

Circular stickers, that are usually green, are found on the bottom right of the windshield on almost every car in Germany. They will have the number 4 written on them. They can rarely be yellow or red instead, and will have the numbers 3 and 2 respectively.

Wind turbines are far more common throughout Germany than in any other European country.

## regional_clues

Using surrounding elevation can be crucial. The north and especially northwest is very flat. Central Germany is hilly. Very southern Germany has big mountains.

German area codes are grouped by their first digit.

Postal codes are arranged in a counter-clockwise fashion, starting with 0 around Saxony and ending with 9 in northern Bavaria and parts of Thuringia.

Each German state has their own flag and coat of arms.

Some of the more useful place name suffixes for regionguessing: -ow and -itz are primarily found in the east; -weiler and -ach are mainly located in the southern half.

While most of Germany uses B-Type guardrails, A-type guardrails are common in the northwest, as well as in Rhineland-Palatinate and parts of Bavaria.

Bicycle direction signs typically feature a white background with green lettering. In Hamburg, North Rhine-Westphalia and a neighbouring part of Lower Saxony, you will instead find red lettering. The Saarland has its own distinctive design, with white text on a dark green background.

Backs of street signs (folded vs flat) and sign back connectors can be used to regionguess Germany.

Landscape and vegetation: The German Alps is the most mountainous region, mostly concentrated on the Austrian Border. Baltic Pines (Pinus sylvestris) are generally more concentrated in the northeast, recognized by their orange-tinged trunk. Trees that are painted white close to the bottom of the trunk can be spotted in the northeast.

The flat plains southeast of Regensburg can be recognised by the slight ridge often visible to the northeast.

Vineyards are mostly concentrated in the southwest of Germany. However, there are a couple regions further east as well.

Hop fields are commonly seen just north of Munich.

Architecture: Brick houses are generally concentrated in northwestern Germany. Thatched roofs and rock walls are most common on the North Frisian Islands as well as mainland Schleswig-Holstein. Houses with black slate walls, resembling fish scales, are commonly found in a large area of central Germany. Large utilitarian apartment buildings ("Commie blocks") are common in all of former East Germany.

Infrastructure: Bollards with darker reflectors than usual are found in certain regions. Some bollards in North Rhine-Westphalia have a green bit at the top — green caps with the text 'LVR' are unique to southern North Rhine-Westphalia. Round wooden bollards with painted top are found in Bavaria. Irregular looking bollards without white encasing on the bottom half are found on K-roads in this region of Lower Saxony. Simple orange posts are most commonly found in Bavaria.

Many trees in Brandenburg and Mecklenburg-Vorpommern have tiny green markers on them.

Bavaria uses white rectangular road markers, divided by black lines into three sections.

Simple rectangular markers with rounded edges are found in Lower Saxony.

Markers with a metallic border around the marker are found in Schleswig-Holstein and Lower Saxony.

Triangular posts: Rhineland-Palatinate and Saxony use unpainted, rocky-looking posts with white plates stuck on each side. Saarland, Thuringia, and Baden-Württemberg use white posts with the same white plates. Baden-Württemberg also uses grey posts. North Rhine-Westphalia uses white posts without white plates on the sides. Hesse barely uses these posts at all.

Basic white plates on bollards can be found in certain states.

Bollards standing on black disks are commonly found in all of eastern Germany.

Rhineland-Palatinate is the only state where the plates on the bollards are orange instead of white.

Baden-Württemberg is the only state where the plates on the bollards can be grey instead of white.

White posts with a red cap are found in all of former East Germany, but most commonly in Saxony-Anhalt.

Fully yellow posts are found in Brandenburg.

Yellow posts with a white plate on them are found in Saxony-Anhalt.

Round concrete poles are almost unique to Former Eastern Germany.

Trident-like pole tops can be found in Bavaria, Saxony-Anhalt, and Baden-Württemberg.

Triangle pole tops similar to Czechia and Slovakia are common in Western Germany.

Pole tops where the middle of the horizontal bar is thicker can be found in all of former East Germany.

Pole tops with a curved horizontal bar can be found in Bavaria, though they are rare.

A rare white and black Generation 4 car can be found in Hamburg and around Frankfurt. NOTE: Same car can also be found around London and in Ireland.

There are two regional Aldi chains: Aldi Süd in the south, and Aldi Nord in the north.

## spotlight

Berlin uses white signs with a grey border, and a separate section for numbers under the street name.

Munich has blue street signs with a white decorative border that does not touch the corners.

Stuttgart uses black signs without a border.

Düsseldorf has black signs with a white border, similar to Munich, with a metallic frame.

Dortmund uses blue signs with a white border and a grey frame.

Essen has blue signs with a very thick metallic frame.

Dresden has blue signs with a unique font.

Hanover uses white signs with a wider than usual gap between the sign and metallic frame.

Nuremberg has blue signs with a unique font and a grey frame.

Wuppertal uses white signs with a unique font.

Mainz has a mix of blue signs and a unique red variant.

Frankfurt uses blue street signs with a metallic border around the sign.

Sorbian, a Slavic language, is spoken near the Polish and Czech border.

Every city state uses a unique and recognisable bus stop. Berlin uses segmented bus stops with a yellow signpost. Hamburg bus stops have a red signpost, route numbers within hexagons. Bremen uses simple bus stops with the bus stop name within a red section. Bremerhaven uses bus stops with a grey section on the opposite side of the signpost.

Every city state has recognisable rubbish bins. Berlin uses bright orange bins. Hamburg uses bright red bins with white speech bubbles. Bremen has green and grey bins with orange speech bubbles. Bremerhaven uses a more generic green design.

Lampposts in Cologne will often feature bright yellow numbers on a black piece of plastic.

A type of half-timbered house with arches at the ground floor is very common in this region of Saxony.

The shrub and sand covered hills are unique to the island of Sylt. Like the rest of the North Frisian Islands, rock walls, brick houses, and thatched roofs are common.

Lowcam coverage can be found around Singen and near Switzerland.

A Generation 3 black car can be found in a safari park southeast of Bielefeld. You can commonly see 4x4s painted with a zebra pattern in the park.

Rare Generation 3 car coverage with an antenna can be found in central Hamburg.
