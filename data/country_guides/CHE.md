## general_clues

By far the most important way to recognize Switzerland is that all car coverage is low-cam. The camera is intentionally set up lower than in most other countries. Recognizable by the significantly bigger car blur, and roads looking wider with objects seen from a lower angle. NOTE: The only other countries that always have low-cam are Japan and Liechtenstein.

Swiss licence plates differ substantially from other European plates. They are fully white and lack the blue strip found in most other European countries. Front plates are extremely short compared to most in Europe. Rear plates can be either long or tall. The red country emblem is on the left, while the canton emblem is on the right.

Swiss bollards are black-and-white with white or grey reflectors. They can be either wedge-shaped or cylindrical with a rounded top. NOTE: Liechtenstein uses almost identical round bollards.

The colour of directional signs indicates which type of road you are on. Main road signs will be blue, while side roads are white. Major place names will frequently appear on blue signs, while smaller places tend to be on white signs.

Swiss pedestrian signs have seven stripes, making it almost unique for Europe. Rather than the typical European square shape, the signs appear as tall blue rectangles. NOTE: Liechtenstein uses identical signs.

Long dashed yellow road lines are a good clue for Switzerland.

Pedestrian crossings are typically painted yellow and exclusive to Switzerland and Liechtenstein within Europe.

Swiss road signs commonly have a round, grey metal border around them. NOTE: Liechtenstein uses almost identical directional signs.

Town entry signs in Switzerland are usually white or blue, with a metal frame around them. On the back, you may often find the distance to larger nearby cities. The abbreviation for the canton can often be seen on the bottom of the sign.

Chevrons are black with a white arrow. You may also rarely find chevrons that are white with a black arrow. NOTE: Austrian chevrons are red and white, or red and yellow.

While the southern half of Switzerland is extremely mountainous, with long, thin valleys between the mountains, the northern half mostly consists of large farmlands on either flat ground or rolling hills.

Swiss and Alpine architecture in general largely consists of stone buildings painted in light and warm colours, often combined with wooden parts and details. Roofs are typically tiled and of a gable type. It is extremely common for windows to have wooden shutters which open horizontally in two parts.

Bus stop signs with thick metal signposts going around them are unique to Switzerland.

## regional_clues

Switzerland has four official languages: German, French, Italian and Romansh. Some areas, mostly near the region borders, are bilingual. NOTE: Unlike Germany and Austria, Swiss German does not use the letter ß.

The text on the speed limit signs varies depending on the official languages in the region.

Cantons are the first-level administrative division of Switzerland. It is very common to find the two-letter abbreviation for the canton you are in, as seen on some town entry signs. NOTE: If you happen to come across an unblurred licence plate, the first two letters correspond to the canton.

The first digits of Swiss postal codes are a good way to regionguess. The general pattern is that the numbers increase from the west to east.

Elevation is an important part of region-guessing Switzerland. The southern half is extremely mountainous, while the northern half mostly has smaller mountains and hills.

A fairly flat landscape with a long chain of medium-sized, forested mountains northwest means you are likely south of the Jura Mountains.

Exposed limestone rocks, protruding from forested hills, are commonly found in the Jura mountains.

Rock walls are mainly found in the Jura mountains, but can also be found in the Alps, particularly in the Italian speaking parts.

Typical Italian architecture in Switzerland implies you are in the Italian-speaking areas in the south, principally the canton of Ticino. Smooth orange-yellow stone walls with multiple floors and orange or brownish tiled roofs. Green and brown Italian-style window shutters are common.

Large houses with thick roofs, shaped like an arch underneath, are common around Bern.

Elaborate timber frames, commonly painted red, are found in the northeast.

Around Appenzell, most houses consist of a large living house connected to a long barn, such that the roof of the living house and the barn are at a 90° angle.

In the canton of Graubünden, you will often come across houses entirely made of stone, with white decorative painted details around windows.

Large town entry signs are sometimes found in the canton of Vaud.

Olive green infrastructure (signposts, sign borders, sign backs) is found in Basel-Landschaft.

In the canton of Valais, you will often see thick wooden bollards, which can be square-shaped or cylindrical.

In place of bollards, reflectors in the canton of Glarus will often be attached to fences following the road.

In Geneva and in the valley between Martigny and Lake Geneva, you can find bike path symbols painted on the road with a simplified person on top of two disconnected wheels. (Elsewhere in the country, the symbol will be a somewhat detailed bike.)

## spotlight

If the area appears hilly but not mountainous, and you catch a glimpse of a large lake to the south with tall mountains on the other side, you are very likely north of Lake Geneva.

Seeing a wide lake to the northeast in a low-elevation landscape means Lake Constance (Bodensee).

The valley of Martigny has the highest density of fruit plantations in the country. Recognized by many small fruit trees in a wide flat valley with large mountains on either side.

Apart from Martigny, fruit trees are most likely near Lake Constance, followed by the area northwest of Lake Geneva.

In the canton of Graubünden, you can find unique and easily recognizable train coverage on the tracks of the "Bernina Express." Runs around most of the canton, all the way down to Tirano in Italy.

Generation 2 coverage is very limited in Switzerland. Mostly near the border between Bern and Valais, and on a few short stretches in Schaffhausen.

Blue street signs with rounded edges on the sides are unique to Sankt Gallen.

Large blue street signs attached to a low double signpost are found in Basel.

Downtown Bern can be recognized by grey toned buildings, typically with four floors and large, somewhat rounded openings lined up on the ground floor.
