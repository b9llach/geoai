## general_clues

Croatia has long, white licence plates. Some plates have a blue strip on the left, some are fully white. Most cars seen in Generation 3 will not feature a blue strip. There is a mix in Generation 4, with blue strips more common on newer cars. NOTE: Blue strips are more common along the coast due to travelling tourists.

Croatian bollards are white with a black rectangle and red or white reflector. From the top, shaped like a triangle. NOTE: Hungarian and Lithuanian bollards look similar but have more rounded edges. Lithuanian bollards have an orange reflector. North Macedonia sometimes uses similar bollards but is only covered in Generation 3.

Croatian is a Slavic language, always written in the Latin alphabet. 'IJE' is common in Croatian whereas Serbian uses only 'E' instead. NOTE: Slovene also features many 'J's. Serbian and Montenegrin are very similar but use both Latin and Cyrillic.

Croatia features chevrons with a red arrow on a yellow or white background.

Croatia has square (B-type) guardrails. NOTE: Croatia is one of the few European countries that use B-type guardrails.

Generation 3 coverage often has a non-standard car blur where the side mirrors of the car are partially visible. By far most common in Croatia. Sometimes you can see a small circular yellow sticker on the car mirror — unique to Croatia.

The majority of Generation 3 coverage was made in autumn. Vegetation in this coverage often has distinct yellowish or orange fall colours. Not found in Generation 4 coverage.

A black generation 4 car that will never have an antenna can be found in Croatia. A white generation 4 car that will never have an antenna can also be found.

Stickers with the letters HR (for Hrvatska, the Croatian name for Croatia) can be found on Croatian cars. Mainly used before EU plates were introduced, less common in Generation 4.

Croatia has direction signs with a yellow background and a black outline. They often have a pencil-shaped tip. NOTE: The other former Yugoslav countries (Slovenia, Serbia, Montenegro, North Macedonia) have similar signs.

Croatian utility poles are cylindrical and made of concrete or wood, often with alternating hooks on the wooden poles (somewhat similar to Latvian poles).

Buildings in Croatia sometimes feature exposed red brick walls. More common in inland areas. NOTE: Also found in Slovenia and Serbia.

Croatia features blue fire hydrants with two diagonal protrusions on either side. Newer fire hydrants in Generation 4 have a smaller top but are still recognisable due to the blue paint. NOTE: Similar fire hydrants can be found in bordering countries, most commonly in Hungary.

Croatia's pedestrian sign features a pedestrian with a belt and a zebra path with 5 stripes. NOTE: The pedestrian on Slovenia's sign does not have a belt.

Karlovačko and Ožujsko are common Croatian beer companies, with advertisements seen around the country. Konzum and Plodine are common supermarket chains. Plodine is exclusive to Croatia, while Konzum also operates in Bosnia.

Croatia does not have Generation 2 car coverage. However, in some historic downtown areas, a Generation 2 trekker camera was used. In some of this trekker coverage, the Google employee's red cap is visible.

## regional_clues

Croatian landlines typically start with a zero followed by the area code.

The north-eastern region of Croatia is mostly flat and features a lot of agriculture. Similar in landscape to Hungary and north-western Serbia.

The region north and west of Zagreb is hilly and agricultural.

The Adriatic coast features rocky mountains similar to those seen in Montenegro or Albania. Shrubs are more common instead of trees along the coast, especially in the south. This coastal region has a lot of islands and peninsulas.

Houses and walls made of beige brick are common on the Adriatic coast. They often feature green shutters. NOTE: Similar architecture can also be found in some regions in Italy. Croatian shutters can also be white, beige etc.

The north-western region of Istria, south of the Italian city of Trieste, sometimes has distinct red soil.

Bilingual street name signs with Croatian and Italian are typically found in Istria, especially in the western cities.

Town names ending with "-ec" are common to see in the northern parts of central Croatia.

## spotlight

Croatia's capital Zagreb has a distinct big city feel. Apartment blocks throughout the city. The northern part is hilly. Zagreb has blue electric trams. NOTE: In addition to regular car coverage, Zagreb has various urban sidewalk trekkers, often made with a Generation 2 camera.

Zagreb and Osijek are the only two cities in Croatia with trams.

The region northeast of Zadar is recognizable as it is flat with a mountain ridge visible in the distance north.

There are a lot of vineyards around the town of Ilok, near the Serbian border, west of Novi Sad. If you see lots of vineyards in a northeastern Croatian landscape, you are most often near Ilok.

Traffic lights in Rijeka are held up by long blue poles that extend out over the road.

The town of Petrinja was severely damaged by an earthquake in 2020. Many buildings can be seen ruined and undergoing repair.

The city of Dubrovnik in the far south of coastal Croatia is famous for its beautifully preserved medieval city walls, which can be seen from many points in and around the Old Town.

Tall cypress trees are commonly found near the southern tip of Croatia.
