## general_clues

Modern French plates are long and white, with blue strips on both sides of the plate. The left strip will be more visible than the right strip. Some older French plates lack the right blue strip, with the rear plate being yellow. NOTE: Italian plates also have two blue strips, equally visible. Italian front plates are considerably shorter.

French is a Romance language closely related to Catalan, Spanish and Italian. Diacritics: accent aigu (é), accent grave (à, è, ù), accent circonflexe (â, ê, î, ô, û), trema (ë, ï, ü, ÿ), ligature (œ), cédille (ç). The -ux suffix is only found in French.

The French word for street is "rue".

The French bollard is extremely recognizable: a round white post with a pointed top and a reflector band going all the way around. The band can be grey or red. Some rare variations have a fully red top. NOTE: A bollard commonly found in Scotland looks similar but has a blunt top instead of pointed.

A different round white bollard, with two reflective indented bands, is unique to France.

Less common wedge-shaped bollards with wide rectangular white reflectors on both the front and back are also unique to France.

Concrete poles with step-like indents are common in France ("ladder" or "waffle" poles). NOTE: Also common in Spain.

Wooden poles and thin octagonal metal poles are very common in rural areas and small towns.

Small blue rectangular markings on utility poles are unique to France. Found on almost all wooden and metallic poles, but not as often on ladder poles.

Diamond shaped pole tops with three wires are commonly called "French poletops". Most frequently on top of ladder poles. NOTE: Within Europe, also common in Spain.

Some variations of the French pole tops have the insulators on top, resembling an arrowhead — these are unique to France.

France has Generation 4 "smallcam" coverage.

Small yellow signs displaying the road number, which typically starts with a "D", are unique to France.

French directional signs are usually white with a black outline slightly offset from the edge. Arrow-like shape, with the black border getting thicker at the point. Highway directions have either a blue or green background. Smaller places and points of interest are frequently written in an italic font.

Town entrance signs in France have a red border with a thin white outline.

French road signs have a red border with a thin white outline. You will often find white rectangular signs with black text below traffic signs. NOTE: Road signs in Spain have a thick red border that goes all the way to the edge.

Many traffic signs in France have flat rectangular signposts. Signposts and signbacks can be dyed with unorthodox colours, more so than in other countries. NOTE: The only other European countries with flat signposts are Portugal, Spain, and Andorra.

Traffic signs with particularly short signposts are very common in France.

France uses chevrons with white arrows on a blue background. They can have one to five arrows. NOTE: The only other European country with this colour scheme is Spain.

Pedestrian crossing signs in France have five stripes and a disconnected head on the person. Less commonly, you can find a design with two dashed lines instead of stripes.

Kilometre markers in France most commonly have a yellow or red semicircle on top of a white rectangle. They can be in waystone or flat sign form.

Typical French roads have long spaced out dashes on the outside.

Middle road lines where each dash consists of two offset blocks are unique to France.

Middle lines with dashes that are longer than usual can be a good clue for France. NOTE: Unusually long dashes are also common in the UK.

Arrows on the road signalling the end of a passing zone can be found in France. NOTE: Other countries with these arrows: Spain, Portugal and Romania, and the UK (with arrows pointing left lane instead of right).

France uses A-type guardrails, usually without reflectors. NOTE: Spanish, Andorran and Belgian guardrails use yellow reflectors.

Roads may be lined by tall rectangles on a stick in the colours of the Austrian flag, which are not found elsewhere.

French architecture largely consists of stone houses, normally either unpainted or painted in greyish or yellow pastel colours. Roofs typically have a simple gable design, with orange tiles or less commonly black slates. Townhouses will often lay wall to wall along the road. France has some of the most diverse regional architecture of any European country.

House numbers in France are most commonly blue, typically written in a serif font with slightly exaggerated shapes. NOTE: Useful when differentiating from Italy, which almost never uses blue house number signs.

French car brands Peugeot, Renault and Citroën together make up almost half of all cars in France.

## regional_clues

This guide operates with two types of administrative divisions: regions (totalling 13 in European France) and 96 departments.

French telephone numbers are 10 digits long, with the first two pairs of digits being two separate area codes. Roughly: 01 in Île-de-France; 02-05 going clockwise from the northwest; 2X in the far north with numbers generally increasing anticlockwise; 9X in far northwest and far southeast.

Each department has an assigned number, arranged mostly alphabetically. This is most useful in postal codes (5 digits, first two represent the department).

Infrastructure: Most departments have unique logos appearing on bus stops, kilometre markers, trail banners, directional signs and signbacks.

A red and yellow logo with an Occitan cross and vertical stripes is the official flag of the Occitanie region.

A flag of red and yellow stripes (Senyera-based) is the official flag of Pyrénées-Orientales.

Roads with a significant red, purple or burgundy tint are most commonly found in an area between Paris and Dijon, Normandy being a notable exception.

Dark green road signposts and backs are most commonly found in Brittany and around Paris, but also various southern regions.

Signbacks in Eure-et-Loir may include a green sticker, the department's logo. Often placed on dark green back.

In Eure-et-Loir, D-roads with a superscript number are most common (D134.10, D121.9 etc.).

A greyish green signback supported by a darker green post is found in three areas: Brittany (Côtes-d'Armor and eastern half of Ille-et-Vilaine), Pyrénées-Atlantiques and rarely near the Alps.

In Pas-de-Calais, road signs with dark green post paired with signback of lighter shade of green (matcha to greenish turquoise).

Road signs where both the post and back are painted brown are most commonly found in Seine-et-Marne, and rarely in Ille-et-Vilaine.

Directional signs in Haute-Garonne often use burgundy red attachments and ornaments. Sometimes paired with yellow signposts.

Most town entry signs in Marne follow a unique format: combined entry/exit into a single, noticeably thicker block.

Street signs in metropolitan area of Paris often say the name of the city.

Cuboid barriers made of stones and mortar are commonly found on Corsica.

Landscape: Tallest mountains are found in the southeast and on the Spanish border. The north and west coast is generally quite flat or rolling, while the southern half is often hillier.

Rural roads surrounded by hedges blocking the view, reminiscent of the British Isles, are primarily found in the northwest, particularly in Normandy.

Stone pines are commonly found along the southern coast.

Forests made up entirely of Maritime pines (pinus pinaster) are found in the southwest of the country (Gironde and Landes departments). The whole area will be fairly flat.

France is the second largest wine producer in the world. Most common wine regions: around Bordeaux, southern coast and Champagne (East of Paris). Other regions include Loire Valley, Alsace and Corsica.

Sunflowers are mostly found in the southwest, particularly around Toulouse.

Wind turbines are considerably more common in the north of the country.

Architecture: Black shingle roofs are primarily found in the northwest, the rough cutoff being the Loire river. Other areas: near the Belgian border, the south-centre, and the Pyrenees.

Brittany has one of the most recognizable architectures: black roofs, edge chimneys, black slate walls and stones surrounding doors and windows. Walls will be either very light coloured or made of stone.

Roofs in the south of the country will often be made of rounded tiles overlapping one another, often varying in colour.

Rows of dark red brick houses, most similar to Flemish architecture in Belgium, are unsurprisingly found near the Belgian border.

In and around Toulouse, mainly in old towns, you will commonly find bright orange brick buildings.

Walls made of uneven cream coloured to orange stones, glued with mortar and often incorporating red brick details can be found in towns surrounding Paris.

The French Riviera mirrors many characteristics of Monaco. Rich areas with planted palms and light coloured multi story buildings with wide balconies.

White houses with often colourful timber framing are a staple of Basque architecture.

Alpine architecture (light coloured base and extensive wooden elements) is found in the Alps. Similar houses in the touristic parts of Pyrenees.

Many elements of Germanic architecture are concentrated in Alsace, such as timber framing and half-hip roof.

Languages: France is home to many minority languages, including Alsatian (Alemannic German dialect), Basque (only European language isolate), Breton (Celtic), and three Romance languages (Catalan, Corsican, Occitan).

Breton is a Celtic language, visually most similar to Welsh. Common letters: d, k, w, ñ. Found on Breton peninsula, with bilingual street signs as east as Rennes. The Breton word for street is "straed".

Towns with the Plou- prefix are concentrated in Northwestern Brittany.

Alsatian, a German dialect, is occasionally found in Alsace. Most bilingual street signs use some variation of "gasse" or "strasse".

Occitan is a Romance language closely related to Catalan, sparsely concentrated in the South of France. Different dialects use slightly different words for street (carrièra in Toulouse, carriero in Avignon).

Bilingual signs in Basque are concentrated in the western half of the Pyrénées-Atlantiques department. Common letters: K, X, Z. The word for street is "karrika".

On the island of Corsica, you may find bilingual signs in Corsican.

The southern department Pyrénées-Orientales (Catalunya Nord) uses Catalan. The word for street is "carrer".

Knowing basic toponymy in France is crucial. Common suffixes have regional distributions.

Many French cities contain the name of the river they lay on, in the format -sur-[river]. Cities with -sur-mer or -plage are found exclusively on the coast.

Non-contiguous regions: Réunion is an overseas department east of Madagascar. Saint-Pierre and Miquelon is south of Newfoundland. Martinique is in the eastern Caribbean. All counted as France for country streaks.

## spotlight

Mansard roofs (French windows) are a very common feature of Parisian architecture. Steep black shingle roofs with inserted dormer windows.

On the Breton island Belle-Île-en-Mer you can find houses typical for Brittany combined with colourful façades and outlines. The entire island was only covered with an often oversaturated Generation 2 camera.

Lavender fields can be found in the southeast.

Salt farms (flooded trenches with grass medians) are found along the Atlantic coast, most notably around Guérande, Noirmoutier, Île de Ré and Île d'Oléron.

Paris, Lyon and Marseille are the three largest cities and the only ones divided into municipal arrondissements (Arrᵗ or Arrt.).

Paris uses an iconic green & Prussian blue street sign design with a top arch.

Lyon's street signs feature four corner bolts and an uninterrupted inner white border that cuts a notch for the arrondissement and each bolt.

Marseille's street signs will usually feature the city's emblem, a variation of the Greek cross.

Small tables with "Arrᵗ" written on a small separate sign are found in Bordeaux.

Montpellier street signs will typically contain a blue 'M' on a white background in the top left corner.

Street signs with a red slanted fleur-de-lis are unique to Lille.

In the outskirts of Bordeaux, you may find square traffic signposts with the Bordeaux shade of red.

On the French Riviera (Côte d'Azur), you can find regular traffic signposts painted azure blue. Cagnes-sur-mer features lighter, powder blue infrastructure.

Thin B type guardrail (typical for Czechia and Slovakia) is sometimes found on highways in the northern half of the country.

On rare occasions, you may find a flag attached to the antenna in Generation 3 coverage.
