## general_clues

Israeli licence plates are yellow with a blue strip on the left. NOTE: While this design is specific for Israel, it is also very common on the West Bank. Jordan and the UAE use white licence plates. Tunisia uses black plates.

Most Generation 3 coverage was taken by a black Street View car with a long antenna. You may also see the black car without an antenna and a silver car with a short antenna.

Two languages: Hebrew (practically only found in Israel and the West Bank) and Arabic (particularly common in the West Bank).

The landscape ranges from a greener Mediterranean landscape in the north, to a very arid landscape in the south.

Architecture tends to consist of bright and warm-coloured stone buildings, often with a blocky appearance. While Mediterranean-style houses with orange tiled roofs are more common than in other Middle Eastern countries, flat roofs are still just as common.

Most roads feature yellow outer lines with white centre lines.

You will very often see striped kerbs: red and white, fairly commonly black and white. Red and yellow and blue and white are also rarely found.

Both A-type and B-type guardrails are common. NOTE: The only other Middle Eastern country where B-type guardrails are common is Turkey.

Chevrons are white on black.

Kilometre markers are white with a thick border, which can either be red, green or blue.

Rather than having a word meaning 'stop' written on them, stop signs have a hand painted on them.

Utility poles commonly feature painted black and white striped bottoms.

Utility poles with a mesh framework are very common.

You can often find a yellow warning sign on poles.

## regional_clues

Notably, southern Israel and most of the West Bank has very sparse coverage.

Generation 4 coverage is only found in Israel, with most featuring a dirty front camera. Generation 4 coverage with a clean front camera is mostly found in and around Tel Aviv.

Northern Israel is usually far more mountainous and green than the rest of Israel.

Central Israel is usually flat and has several cities. Highways are usually in better condition than roads in the south and north.

Southern Israel and eastern West Bank are extremely dry and sandy.

The area west of Jerusalem can often look deceptively similar to northern Israel, with green forested hills and some agriculture. Northern Israel may show wide flat valleys, while Jerusalem area is always hilly. Jerusalem stone houses are a tell for Jerusalem area.

Most of the West Bank has a semi-arid Mediterranean landscape. Greener than southern Israel, drier than northern Israel and west of Jerusalem, hillier and less agricultural than central Israel.

In Jerusalem and surrounding cities in the West Bank, nearly every building is built with the same type of material called Jerusalem stone. The stone will look much smoother on newer buildings.

Date palm plantations can often be seen close to the Jordanian border.

Rock walls put together with cement are found in the north of Israel.

The Dead Sea can easily be recognised by the dryness surrounding it. Also visible from Jordan and the West Bank.

Other than the Dead Sea and the Mediterranean Sea, the only major body of water is the Sea of Galilee. Noticeably more vegetated than the Dead Sea.

Mount Carmel is a long mountain ridge near the city of Haifa, going northwest to southeast. There is also a lot of coverage on top of the mountain range, generally fairly green.

Mount Gilboa is a very dry looking mountain, visible from far away.

Small irrigation canals on the side of the road are found in the area around Beit She'an.

Licence plates in the West Bank are often white with green text. Public transport vehicles have inverted colours: green with white text.

A significant portion of the coverage in the West Bank has a visible security follow car. Most extensive: a small white Suzuki in central and southern regions. Another follow car: a small dark blue Ford in the northwest. A larger white Citroën follow car with West Bank licence plate around Jerusalem (Bethlehem and Ramallah).

## spotlight

Road 90 is the longest road in Israel, often recognised by its dryness combined with the general north to south angle. The southern stretch will have a dry mountain range to the east, and palm trees are common.

Road 2 can usually be recognised by the Mediterranean sea visible on the west side. If you see mountains to the east, you will be somewhere between Haifa and Zikhron Yaakov.

Mount Tavor in the Northern District can be recognised by its soft, round shape.

Timna National park can be recognised by the complete dryness combined with mountain ridges in all directions.

Tall, rocky mountains east of Eilat make the city very easy to recognize.

Mountain range in the far north resembling a camel's back. If you see these mountains to the west and you're in a city, you will be in Kiryat Shmona.

North of the Hasolelim forest reserve is a large reservoir.

Finding parking in Tel Aviv is notoriously difficult — the side of almost every road is full of parked cars.

White rectangular signs with a thin blue border are typically found in Jerusalem.

Jericho can commonly be recognised by date palms in a somewhat sparsely built and poor-looking urban setting.

In Ramallah you will generally see several black water tanks on most houses. The sheer number of them on most buildings is what makes it stand out.

Grey mosaic brick work shaped like towers is unique to Harish.

A white lamp with three arms and round light bulbs on top is unique to Nahariya.

Lamps shaped like Barad Dûr are unique to Acre.

Metal lamps with one high and one low lamp put together with very angular look are unique to Netivot.

Poles with blue and white paint on the bottom are unique to Eilat.

Yellow fences and signposts are unique to Kiryat Motzkin.

Blue and white fence is very common in Ashdod.

Bethlehem: Blue house numbers with the number written with Arabic numerals in the top left and Eastern Arabic numerals in the bottom right, with a thin diagonal line between them.

House numbers in Rosh Haayin are blue, with a G-clef in the bottom left corner.

In Ness Ziona, there is an Israeli flag on house numbers.

Blue, shield-shaped house numbers are unique to Herzliya.

Bnei Brak is an ultra-Orthodox Jewish stronghold — usually you will see people wearing Jewish religious clothes.

Roundabout signs which are very thick or attached to a large cube can be found in Raanana.

Tall industrial chimneys by the coast north of Netanya are visible from far away.

A grey Hyundai follow car is unique to Rahat.

The Israel National Trail goes through the entire country with one of the longest trekkers in the world. Goes through every landscape from the rocky desert in the south to lush forests in the north. Trail markings typically orange, blue and white.
