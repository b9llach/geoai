## general_clues

Finland has shorter than normal white plates with the standard European blue strip on the left. NOTE: Åland has small, fully white plates with blue lettering.

Where present, outer road lines will always be solid white lines. NOTE: Unlike Sweden and Norway, Finland does not use dashed outside lines. The only exception are the Åland Islands.

Finland has several different types of middle road lines: white dashes, with or without a solid white line next to it; solid yellow with white dashes; double yellow. Yellow lines are being phased out and increasingly rare in newer coverage. NOTE: Sweden never has yellow middle lines. Norway has middle lines with a distinct orange tint.

Finland has long black-and-white bollards with a rectangular, white reflector on the front and two dots on the back. They can be either round or thin and curved. The black strip is typically diagonal.

In Finland, it is common to find orange snow poles with a thin white band near the top. NOTE: Also common in Norway and Sweden, but with a much wider white band, lower down on the pole.

The Finnish language is not related to most other European languages. It has a lot of long words and double vowels. The Finnish alphabet includes the letters ä and ö. NOTE: Estonian is closely related to Finnish. Estonian uses õ and ü, which are not used in Finnish.

Most Finnish street names end in "tie" or "katu" (Finnish words for road and street). Street signs most commonly have a white background with black text. NOTE: You may find roads ending in "polku" and "kuja".

Finnish road signs have a yellow background. NOTE: Signs in Sweden and Iceland also have yellow backgrounds. Norway has signs with white background.

On traffic signs that have a red border, you will find a thin yellow stroke on the edge of the sign. NOTE: You will not see this in any other Nordic country.

Finnish pedestrian signs have 5 stripes. NOTE: Pedestrian signs in Sweden and Norway usually have 4 stripes.

Finnish directional signs consist of a solid blue background, white text, and a white border with guiding arrows.

Finnish chevrons are black with yellow arrows. NOTE: Norway and Iceland have similarly coloured chevrons. Sweden has unique blue and yellow chevrons.

Finland primarily uses wooden utility poles. Most have a simple design but can have a variety of poletops. Poles often have a street light attached.

Lamps attached to wooden poles in Finland are normally mounted to the top of the pole with a double bolting. NOTE: Sweden also uses double bolting, but lamps are mounted further down and lamp arms are usually not straight.

Traffic signposts often have blue and white striped markings. NOTE: This pattern can also be found in Sweden.

Most of Finland is covered by dense boreal forests. Pine trees are common, as well as birches, easily recognizable by their white trunks.

A lot of Generation 3 coverage was taken in autumn, meaning that trees often have distinct fall colours. NOTE: This is significantly less common in the other Nordics. A fall vibe can be a good clue for Finland.

Lakes are so common in Finland that the country is also sometimes known as the Land of a Thousand Lakes.

Finnish architecture is mostly modern and utilitarian. Suburbs mostly consist of freestanding single family homes. Many houses have wooden cladding. Like Sweden and Norway, some wooden buildings, especially in the countryside, are painted in a distinctive dark red colour.

Finland has two styles of bus stop signs: a blue and white one, and a yellow and black one. NOTE: Distinguishes from Sweden, which has many different bus stop signs.

K-Market and S-Market are two Finnish supermarket chains operating only in Finland. K-Market is sometimes known as K-Supermarket or K-Citymarket.

Finland has "Shitcam" coverage with a wide circular car blur. Within Europe, Finland and Spain are the countries with most coverage of this type.

Finland also has Generation 4 "smallcam" coverage.

## regional_clues

Finnish regional roads have 3- or 4-digit road numbers. The first digit is region-specific, distributed roughly south-to-north (1 in south, 9 in north).

Snow poles with 2 long reflectors can be found on bigger roads in Lapland. NOTE: Only in coverage from 2023 onward.

Finland has a lot of farmlands, but the majority are concentrated near the west and south coast.

Wind turbines are most commonly found along the west coast of Finland.

Small, stunted trees with spindly, thin trunks are common in northern Finland.

Generation 3 snow coverage is only found in far northwestern Lapland. NOTE: Generation 2, 4, and trekker snow coverage can be found elsewhere.

The Swedish language is common in some coastal areas in the south and the west. In these areas, bilingual Swedish or Finnish texts are common. If you see Swedish language in a Finnish setting, do not guess inland.

The Sámi languages are spoken in the far north of Finland. Bilingual signs with place names in both Finnish and Sámi. Unlike Finnish, its alphabet uses the letter c.

A slightly tilted, long antenna can be found in southwestern Finland.

Generation 3 coverage with no antenna at all can be found in south-central Finland.

Åland Islands: a group of islands off the southwestern coast. Autonomous, mostly self-governing region of Finland with its own flag. NOTE: Åland has no Generation 4 coverage. For country streaks purposes still counted as part of Finland.

Åland is monolingually Swedish. If you see only Swedish, and no Finnish language, but you suspect Finland, consider the Åland Islands.

Åland roads have two unique aspects: pavement tends to have a red tint, and they have dashed outside road lines that resemble those found in Sweden.

Licence plates on Åland are white with blue text, making them appear almost completely white on Street View. They are noticeably quite short and lack the European blue strip on the left side.

Some pedestrian signs on Åland have four stripes, with the exact same design found in Sweden.

Bus stops on Åland include a white plate with 4 numbers. Some also include a semicircle sign on top.

## spotlight

Road 970 in the far north, on the border with Norway, runs through a river valley with hills on both sides. The river is visible in many places, usually to the northwest. The birch trees around the road are small and stunted. NOTE: Generation 3 coverage taken in autumn — vegetation looks dead and devoid of leaves.

Due to its proximity to the Scandinavian Mountains, the area near Kilpisjärvi features a unique hilly landscape, with low vegetation.

Downtown Helsinki has a lot of Generation 4 sidewalk trekker coverage.

Helsinki features unique green-and-yellow trams. NOTE: Tampere is the only other Finnish city with trams, but they are red instead of green and yellow.

The buses in and around Helsinki are typically blue-and-white, with a vertical split.

The buses in and around Tampere are blue on bottom and white on top. The word 'NYSSE' is written on the sides.

Buses in Turku are uniquely yellow, with a white rear.

Buses in Jyväskylä are almost completely green.

Buses in Oulu have a pink front.

Red rectangular metallic plates with 7 golden or dark red coloured protrusions forming a rounded hexagon shape are found on houses, lamp posts, and signposts in Tampere.

Blue street signs with white text are unique to Seinäjoki and Isokyrö southeast of Vaasa.

Lahti uses street signs with a black border and all capital letters.

Kerava uses street signs with a blue-and-yellow coat of arms in the top-left corner.

Järvenpää uses street signs with a blue-and-grey coat of arms in the top-left corner. The neighbourhood name is also written above the street name.
